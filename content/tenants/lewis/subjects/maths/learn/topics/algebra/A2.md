### What this is about

[Substitution](../glossary/substitute.md) means **replace each letter with a number** you are told, then work out the value using normal arithmetic.

If $x = 4$, then $x + 3$ becomes $4 + 3 = 7$. The letter was a placeholder; once you know its value, you calculate like any other sum.

You might substitute into simple sums, into expressions with powers, or into [formulae](../glossary/formula.md) with more than one letter. Always write the number in brackets when you replace a letter — especially when multiplying — so $2x$ with $x = 5$ becomes $2 × 5$, not 25 by mistake.

Substitution is how you check whether an [equation](../glossary/equation.md) is true for a particular value, and how you use rules in science and sport (plug in time, distance, or speed and get an answer).

### Words to know

- **[Substitute](../glossary/substitute.md)** — swap a letter for a given number.
- **[Expression](../glossary/expression.md)** — a calculation with letters, like $3a + 2$ (no equals sign).
- **Value** — the number you get after substituting and calculating.
- **Term** — one part of an expression, such as $5n$ or $-2$.
- **Coefficient** — the number multiplying a letter, as 5 in $5n$.
- **[Formula](../glossary/formula.md)** — an equation that links quantities, like $A = lw$ for area.

### Worked example 1

If $x = 6$, find $2x + 5$.

Replace $x$: $2(6) + 5 = 12 + 5 = 17$.

**Answer:** 17.

### Worked example 2

If $a = 3$ and $b = 4$, find $a^2 + 2ab$.

Substitute: $3^2 + 2 × 3 × 4 = 9 + 24 = 33$.

**Answer:** 33.

Remember: $2ab$ means $2 × a × b$, not “2 and ab added strangely”.

### Tips for practice

- Write each substitution step — do not skip straight to the answer in your head if brackets are involved.
- Use brackets when you substitute: $3x$ with $x = -2$ is $3 × (-2)$.
- Powers first: in $n^2 + n$ with $n = 5$, work $5^2$ then add 5.
- Negative numbers need extra care with minus signs.
