### What this is about

**Simplifying** means writing an [expression](../glossary/expression.md) in a shorter, cleaner form — usually by **collecting like terms**. $3x + 5x$ becomes $8x$ because both terms are “lots of $x$”. You cannot combine $3x + 5y$ into one term because the letters differ.

**Expanding** means **removing brackets** by multiplying every term inside by the number outside. $4(y + 2)$ becomes $4y + 8$. This uses the distributive law: $4 × y$ and $4 × 2$ separately.

You might expand first, then simplify — or simplify inside brackets before expanding. These skills keep equations tidy before you solve them, and they show that $2(x + 3)$ really means $2x + 6$, not $2x + 3$.

### Words to know

- **Simplify** — combine like terms and write more neatly.
- **Expand** — multiply out brackets.
- **Like terms** — same variable(s), e.g. $7a$ and $-2a$.
- **Unlike terms** — different letters or powers, e.g. $x$ and $x^2$.
- **Distributive law** — $a(b + c) = ab + ac$.
- **Coefficient** — the number multiplying the letter.

### Worked example 1

Simplify $5x + 2 - 3x + 7$.

Group $x$ terms: $5x - 3x = 2x$. Group numbers: $2 + 7 = 9$.

**Answer:** $2x + 9$.

### Worked example 2

Expand and simplify $3(2x - 4) + 2(x + 1)$.

First bracket: $6x - 12$. Second bracket: $2x + 2$.

Add: $6x - 12 + 2x + 2 = 8x - 10$.

**Answer:** $8x - 10$.

### Tips for practice

- Every term **inside** the bracket gets multiplied — including the minus sign on the second term.
- $-(x - 3)$ expands to $-x + 3$, not $-x - 3$.
- After expanding, always collect like terms again.
- Check with a number: pick $x = 1$ and compare original and simplified forms.
