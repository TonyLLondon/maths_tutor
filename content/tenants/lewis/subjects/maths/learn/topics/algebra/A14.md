### What this is about

Some graphs tell a **story** about the real world — not just $x$ and $y$ on a grid. A **distance–time graph** shows how far something has travelled and when. Time is usually on the horizontal axis; distance on the vertical.

A **flat (horizontal) section** means distance is not changing — the object is **stationary**. A **steep rising** section means moving **quickly**; a **gentle slope** means slower. A **downward** section on distance–time would mean going **backwards** toward the start (unusual but possible on some problems).

Other contexts use similar ideas: height of water in a tank, temperature over a day, or cost against number of items. The skill is linking **shape** to **meaning**: what is happening in the story when the graph is flat, steep, or curved?

### Words to know

- **Distance–time graph** — distance from start against time elapsed.
- **Stationary** — not moving; flat section on distance–time.
- **Speed** — how fast distance changes; linked to steepness (gradient) on distance–time.
- **Rate of change** — how quickly one quantity grows as another grows.
- **[Line graph](../glossary/line-graph.md)** — points joined to show change over time.

### Worked example 1

A walker rests for 10 minutes, then walks 2 km in 20 minutes. Describe the graph shape.

Start at distance 0. **Flat line** for 10 minutes (rest). Then **straight rising line** for 20 minutes until distance reaches 2 km. Total time shown: 30 minutes.

### Worked example 2

On a distance–time graph, two journeys both end at 5 km. Journey A takes 10 minutes; journey B takes 25 minutes. Which is faster?

Journey A reaches 5 km in less time — the line is **steeper**. **Journey A is faster.**

### Tips for practice

- Read the **axis labels** first — know what each axis measures and the units.
- Flat on distance–time ≠ “slow”; it means **stopped**.
- Steeper rise ⇒ greater speed on a distance–time graph.
- A curved graph can still make sense — speed might be changing — but describe it in words carefully.
