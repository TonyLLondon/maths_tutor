### What this is about

A **straight-line graph** links an $x$-value to a $y$-value so that all the points lie on a **line**. Rules like $y = x + 2$ or $y = -x + 1$ produce straight lines when you plot enough points.

You can build a **table** of values: pick $x = -1, 0, 1, 2$, work out each $y$, plot the points, and draw the line through them. If the rule is linear, two points are enough to draw the line — but a third point is a good check.

The graph shows every pair that satisfies the rule at once. Reading the graph backwards lets you estimate $x$ when you know $y$, or see whether a point like $(3, 7)$ lies on the line.

Straight-line graphs model steady rates: so much per hour, so much per mile, a fixed start plus a fixed step each time.

### Words to know

- **Linear graph** — a straight line from a rule $y = mx + c$.
- **Table of values** — list of matching $x$ and $y$ pairs.
- **Plot** — mark a point from its [coordinates](../glossary/coordinates.md).
- **Axis intercept** — where the line crosses an axis (often the $y$-intercept when $x = 0$).
- **Gradient** — how steep the line is (covered in more detail in the next topic).

### Worked example 1

Complete a table for $y = 2x - 1$ when $x = 0, 1, 2, 3$.

$x = 0$ → $y = -1$. $x = 1$ → $y = 1$. $x = 2$ → $y = 3$. $x = 3$ → $y = 5$.

Plot $(0,-1), (1,1), (2,3), (3,5)$ and draw the line through them.

### Worked example 2

Does $(4, 9)$ lie on $y = 2x + 1$?

Substitute $x = 4$: $y = 2(4) + 1 = 9$. The $y$-value matches.

**Answer:** yes, the point is on the line.

### Tips for practice

- Always substitute into the **right side** of $y = …$ to find $y$.
- Use at least three points, including a negative $x$ if the grid allows.
- Use a ruler for the line — sketching freehand can look curved by mistake.
- If points do not line up, recheck your table arithmetic.
