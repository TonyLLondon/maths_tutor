### What this is about

Every non-vertical straight line has a **[gradient](../glossary/gradient.md)** — how much $y$ changes when $x$ increases by 1. Steep uphill lines have a larger positive gradient; downhill lines have a negative gradient.

From two points on a line, gradient = **change in $y$** ÷ **change in $x$** (rise over run). From $(1, 2)$ to $(4, 8)$, $y$ rises 6 while $x$ rises 3, so gradient = $6 ÷ 3 = 2$.

The **$y$-intercept** is where the line crosses the $y$-axis, at $x = 0$. In $y = mx + c$, the number **$c$** is the $y$-intercept and **$m$** is the gradient. So $y = 3x - 2$ has gradient 3 and crosses at $(0, -2)$.

Gradient and intercept tell you the story of a line quickly — speed of change and starting value — without plotting every point.

### Words to know

- **[Gradient](../glossary/gradient.md)** — slope; $\dfrac{\text{change in } y}{\text{change in } x}$.
- **$y$-intercept** — value of $y$ when $x = 0$.
- **$x$-intercept** — value of $x$ when $y = 0$ (where the line crosses the $x$-axis).
- **Parallel lines** — same gradient, different intercepts.
- **Perpendicular lines** — gradients multiply to $-1$ (extension level).

### Worked example 1

Find the gradient of the line through $(0, 1)$ and $(2, 7)$.

Change in $y$ = $7 - 1 = 6$. Change in $x$ = $2 - 0 = 2$.

Gradient = $\dfrac{6}{2} = 3$.

### Worked example 2

Write the equation of a line with gradient $-2$ and $y$-intercept 5.

Use $y = mx + c$ with $m = -2$ and $c = 5$.

**Answer:** $y = -2x + 5$.

Check: when $x = 0$, $y = 5$ ✓

### Tips for practice

- Keep $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ in the **same order** — end minus start for both.
- Going left to right, if the line falls, the gradient is negative.
- In $y = mx + c$, do not mix up $m$ and $c$.
- Draw a small triangle on the graph to count rise and run.
