### What this is about

Sometimes you need **two unknowns at once** — like the price of an apple and a banana, or two numbers that fit two different clues. A **pair of equations** with two unknowns (often $x$ and $y$) is called **simultaneous equations**. You are looking for values that make **both** equations true at the same time.

One equation alone might have many answers. Two good equations together usually pin down **one** answer pair. You might **substitute** — replace one letter with an expression from the other equation. Or you **eliminate** — add or subtract the equations so one unknown disappears, then solve for the other.

These methods show up in shop totals, ticket prices, and geometry when two lengths are linked. The same balance ideas as linear equations still apply; you are just juggling two rules together.

### Words to know

- **Simultaneous equations** — two (or more) equations true for the same unknowns at once.
- **Unknown** — the letter you are trying to find, e.g. $x$ or $y$.
- **Substitute** — swap a letter for an equal [expression](../glossary/expression.md) or number from another equation.
- **Eliminate** — remove one unknown by adding or subtracting whole equations.
- **Solution pair** — the $(x, y)$ values that satisfy both equations.

### Worked example 1

Solve: $x + y = 10$ and $x - y = 2$.

Add the equations: $(x + y) + (x - y) = 10 + 2$, so $2x = 12$ and $x = 6$.

Substitute into $x + y = 10$: $6 + y = 10$, so $y = 4$.

**Answer:** $x = 6$, $y = 4$. Check in both: $6 + 4 = 10$ ✓ and $6 - 4 = 2$ ✓

### Worked example 2

Solve: $y = 2x$ and $x + y = 12$.

Replace $y$ in the second equation: $x + 2x = 12$, so $3x = 12$ and $x = 4$.

Then $y = 2(4) = 8$.

**Answer:** $x = 4$, $y = 8$.

### Tips for practice

- Label what $x$ and $y$ mean in a word problem before you write equations.
- When coefficients match, **subtract** to eliminate; when one is the negative of the other, **add**.
- After finding one unknown, always find the second and **check both** original equations.
- If one equation already says $y = \ldots$ or $x = \ldots$, substitution is often quickest.
