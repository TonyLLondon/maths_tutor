### What this is about

A **quadratic equation** has an **$x^2$** term (and no higher power). Simple ones look like $x^2 = 49$ — take square roots to get $x = 7$ or $x = -7$ unless the problem asks for positive answers only.

Many quadratics factorise: $x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0$ becomes $(x + 2)(x + 3) = 0$. If two things multiply to zero, at least one must be zero — so $x + 2 = 0$ or $x + 3 = 0$, giving $x = -2$ or $x = -3$.

Some equations need **rearranging** to zero first: $x^2 = 3x$ → $x^2 - 3x = 0$ → $x(x - 3) = 0$. Quadratic equations often have **two** solutions, which matches the U-shaped graph crossing the $x$-axis twice (or once, or not at all).

### Words to know

- **Quadratic equation** — includes $x^2$; standard form often $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.
- **Factorise** — write as a product of brackets, e.g. $(x + 4)(x - 1)$.
- **Zero product rule** — if $AB = 0$, then $A = 0$ or $B = 0$.
- **Square root** — undo squaring; remember both positive and negative roots when appropriate.
- **Root / solution** — a value of $x$ that satisfies the equation.

### Worked example 1

Solve $x^2 = 81$.

$x = \sqrt{81}$ or $x = -\sqrt{81}$ → $x = 9$ or $x = -9$.

### Worked example 2

Solve $x^2 - x - 12 = 0$.

Factorise: $(x - 4)(x + 3) = 0$. So $x = 4$ or $x = -3$.

Check $x = 4$: $16 - 4 - 12 = 0$ ✓

### Tips for practice

- Rearrange to **= 0** before factorising when terms are on both sides.
- Find two numbers that **multiply to $c$** and **add to $b$** for $x^2 + bx + c$.
- If $x^2$ has a coefficient other than 1, factorise carefully or use another method from your worksheet.
- Context problems may reject a negative solution (length cannot be negative).
