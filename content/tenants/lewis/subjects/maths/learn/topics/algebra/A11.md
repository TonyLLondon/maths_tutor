### What this is about

A **quadratic graph** comes from a rule with **$x^2$** — the highest power of $x$ is 2. The simplest is $y = x^2$. It is **not** a straight line; it curves into a **U-shape** called a **parabola**.

Because squaring makes negative and positive $x$ give the same square ($(-3)^2 = 9$ and $3^2 = 9$), the graph is **symmetric** about a vertical line through its lowest (or highest) point, the **vertex**.

Adding a constant shifts the U up or down: $y = x^2 + 3$ sits 3 units above $y = x^2$. Multiplying $x^2$ by a number stretches the U narrower or wider.

Quadratic graphs model paths of balls, areas that depend on side length, and any situation where “doubling $x$” does not double $y$ — the rate of change itself changes.

### Words to know

- **Quadratic** — involving $x^2$ as the highest power.
- **Parabola** — the U-shaped curve of $y = ax^2 + bx + c$.
- **Vertex** — the turning point (minimum or maximum).
- **Axis of symmetry** — vertical line where the two arms mirror each other.
- **Minimum / maximum** — lowest or highest $y$ on the parabola (for the usual U or ∩ shapes).

### Worked example 1

Make a table for $y = x^2$ for $x = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2$.

$(-2)^2 = 4$, $(-1)^2 = 1$, $0^2 = 0$, $1^2 = 1$, $2^2 = 4$.

Plot and draw a smooth U through the points. The bottom is at $(0, 0)$.

### Worked example 2

For $y = x^2 - 4$, find where the graph crosses the $x$-axis.

Set $y = 0$: $x^2 - 4 = 0$, so $x^2 = 4$, $x = 2$ or $x = -2$.

**Crossings:** $(2, 0)$ and $(-2, 0)$. The vertex is at $(0, -4)$.

### Tips for practice

- Always square the **whole** $x$, including negatives: $(-3)^2 = 9$.
- Plot points on **both sides** of zero to see the symmetry.
- Join points with a smooth curve, not straight segments.
- Compare $y = x^2$ and $y = x^2 + k$ — same shape, shifted vertically.
