### What this is about

Equations come in **families** — groups that share the same overall shape on a graph.

**Linear:** $y = mx + c$ → **straight line**. **Quadratic:** $y = ax^2 + …$ → **parabola (U)**. **Cubic:** $y = x^3$ → an **S-shaped** curve through the origin. **Reciprocal:** $y = \dfrac{k}{x}$ → **two branches** that never touch the axes (hyperbola shape).

Recognising the family helps you sketch quickly and predict behaviour: a linear rule grows steadily; a quadratic eventually rises fast on both sides; $y = \dfrac{1}{x}$ cannot equal 0.

You compare graphs by how they transform — shifts up, stretches, reflections — without plotting every single point.

### Words to know

- **Linear family** — rules with $x$ to power 1 only.
- **Quadratic family** — highest power $x^2$.
- **Cubic family** — highest power $x^3$.
- **Reciprocal graph** — $y = \dfrac{k}{x}$, two separate curves.
- **Asymptote** — a line the graph approaches but does not reach (often axes for reciprocal graphs).

### Worked example 1

Name the family and shape of $y = 5x - 1$ and $y = 2x^2 + 3$.

$y = 5x - 1$ is **linear** → straight line. $y = 2x^2 + 3$ is **quadratic** → parabola opening upward.

### Worked example 2

Sketch the idea of $y = \dfrac{6}{x}$ without a calculator.

For positive $x$, $y$ is positive and gets smaller as $x$ grows. For negative $x$, $y$ is negative. The graph never crosses $x = 0$ or $y = 0$.

**Two smooth branches** in quadrants I and III (for $k > 0$).

### Tips for practice

- Highest power of $x$ in the rule usually tells you the family.
- $y = x^3$ passes through the origin and is steeper than $y = x$ for large $|x|$.
- Reciprocal graphs are **not** one connected loop — two parts.
- When comparing, try $x = 1, 2, -1$ in a quick table.
