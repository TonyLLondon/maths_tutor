### What this is about

A **coordinate grid** has a horizontal **$x$-axis** and a vertical **$y$-axis**. They cross at the **origin** $(0, 0)$. Every point is written as **$(x, y)$** — $x$ first (left/right), then $y$ (up/down).

When you include **negative** numbers, the grid splits into **four quadrants**. Quadrant I has both $x$ and $y$ positive (top right). Moving left makes $x$ negative; moving down makes $y$ negative. So $(-3, 2)$ is 3 left and 2 up; $(-2, -4)$ is 2 left and 4 down.

[Coordinates](../glossary/coordinates.md) describe positions on maps, screens, and graphs. Before you plot $y = 2x + 1$, you need to read and write points correctly — including below and left of the origin.

### Words to know

- **[Coordinates](../glossary/coordinates.md)** — an ordered pair $(x, y)$ locating a point.
- **Origin** — where axes meet, $(0, 0)$.
- **$x$-axis** — horizontal number line.
- **$y$-axis** — vertical number line.
- **Quadrant** — one of four regions around the origin (often numbered I–IV).
- **Positive / negative direction** — right and up are positive on the usual school grid.

### Worked example 1

Write the coordinates of a point 4 units right and 3 units down from the origin.

Right → $x = 4$. Down → $y = -3$.

**Answer:** $(4, -3)$.

### Worked example 2

Plot and describe $(-2, 5)$ and $(-1, -3)$.

$(-2, 5)$: 2 left, 5 up (quadrant II). $(-1, -3)$: 1 left, 3 down (quadrant III).

**Check:** always $x$ before $y$ — $(5, -2)$ is a different point from $(-2, 5)$.

### Tips for practice

- “Along the corridor, up the stairs” — $x$ then $y$.
- Negative $x$ means left; negative $y$ means down.
- On a blank grid, label the origin and both axes before plotting.
- When reading a point, count squares carefully from the origin, not from the edge of the paper.
