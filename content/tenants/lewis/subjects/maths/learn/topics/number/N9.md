### What this is about

**[Standard form](../glossary/standard-form.md)** is a neat way to write very **large** or very **small** numbers. You write a number between 1 and 10 (including 1, but not 10) times a power of 10.

Examples: $4000 = 4 \times 10^3$. $0.006 = 6 \times 10^{-3}$. The index counts how many places the decimal point moves.

**Positive** index → big number (point moves right). **Negative** index → small number (point moves left).

Scientists use standard form for distances in space and sizes of atoms. It also makes comparing sizes easier: which is bigger, $3.2 \times 10^5$ or $7 \times 10^4$?

Compare the powers of 10 first when the indices differ. If the indices are equal, compare the $a$ parts ($6.2 \times 10^5$ beats $5.9 \times 10^5$).

To convert back to an ordinary number, expand the power of 10.

Writing $72{,}000$ in standard form: move the point left until you have 7.2 → $7.2 \times 10^4$. For $0.000045$, move right to 4.5 → $4.5 \times 10^{-5}$. Count moves carefully — one wrong jump changes the index by one.

### Words to know

- **[Standard form](../glossary/standard-form.md)** — $a \times 10^n$ with $1 \le a < 10$.
- **Ordinary number** — usual decimal form.
- **Power of 10** — $10^1 = 10$, $10^2 = 100$, $10^{-1} = 0.1$, etc.
- **[Decimal](../glossary/decimal.md)** — place value moves when you multiply or divide by 10.

### Worked example 1

Write 350,000 in standard form.

Move the point left 5 places: $3.5 \times 10^5$.

### Worked example 2

Write $5 \times 10^{-3}$ as an ordinary number.

Negative 3 means move the point 3 places left from 5: **0.005**.

Write 0.00081 as standard form: $8.1 \times 10^{-4}$ (leading digit 8.1, four jumps to reach 0.00081).

### Tips for practice

- The $a$ part must be at least 1 and less than 10 — not 35, not 0.35.
- Count jumps from the new position of the decimal to the original — that is $n$.
- For tiny decimals, expect a **negative** index.
- Compare powers of 10 first when deciding which standard-form number is larger.
- When writing very small numbers, the leading digit in $a$ is **not** zero — move until you have 1–9 before the point.
- Ordinary → standard: count jumps; standard → ordinary: write the zeros or move the decimal the other way.
