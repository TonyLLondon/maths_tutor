### What this is about

“Find $\dfrac{3}{4}$ of 20” means multiply: $\dfrac{3}{4} \times 20 = 15$. The [fraction](../glossary/fraction.md) acts as an **operator** on the amount.

“Find 25% of 80” means the same style: [percentage](../glossary/percentage.md) is a fraction out of 100. $25\% = \dfrac{25}{100} = \dfrac{1}{4}$, so $\dfrac{1}{4} \times 80 = 20$.

Quick percentage facts help: 10% is one tenth (move decimal one place). 50% is half. 1% is one hundredth.

Sale prices use this: 20% off £50 → find 20% of 50, then subtract (or find 80% of 50).

You can also increase an amount: 10% VAT on £30 → find 10% of 30 and add. The words **of**, **off**, and **increase** tell you whether to take a fraction of the original or adjust the total.

Remember: “find 20%” is not the same as “add 20” unless the amount is £100.

### Words to know

- **Of** — usually means multiply when a fraction or % is in front.
- **[Fraction](../glossary/fraction.md)** — operator × amount.
- **[Percentage](../glossary/percentage.md)** — per hundred; convert to fraction or decimal first.
- **10%** — divide by 10.
- **Increase / decrease** — add or subtract the fraction or % of the original.

### Worked example 1

Work out $\dfrac{2}{3}$ of 27.

$\dfrac{2}{3} \times 27 = \dfrac{54}{3} = 18$.

Or: one third of 27 is 9; two thirds is 18.

### Worked example 2

Work out 15% of £40.

10% of 40 = £4. 5% is half of that = £2. Total 15% = **£6**.

Alternatively: $0.15 \times 40 = 6$.

Find $\dfrac{3}{8}$ of 64: $\dfrac{3}{8} \times 64 = 3 \times 8 = 24$.

### Tips for practice

- “Of” → multiply; “off” a price → subtract that amount (or multiply by the remaining %).
- Build from 10% for mental percentages.
- For fractions of amounts, divide by the bottom then multiply by the top.
- Half price = 50% of the original, not subtract 50 pence unless the price is £1.
- Chain discounts are tricky: 20% then 10% off is **not** 30% off the start price.
- Non-calculator: split percentages into 10% chunks you know.
