### What this is about

A **[ratio](../glossary/ratio.md)** compares parts of a group, like red to blue counters is 3 : 7. The **whole** is all the parts added together.

To find “what [fraction](../glossary/fraction.md) of the whole is one part?”, add the ratio numbers for the **total parts**, then use one part as the numerator.

Example: boys : girls = 2 : 3. Total parts = 5. Fraction of girls = $\dfrac{3}{5}$.

Sharing money in a ratio uses the same idea: find each share, then express one share as a fraction of the total. This links to **[proportion](../glossary/proportion.md)** — the fraction stays the same if the group grows evenly.

### Words to know

- **[Ratio](../glossary/ratio.md)** — comparison of parts with a colon (e.g. 1 : 3).
- **[Fraction](../glossary/fraction.md)** — part of the whole.
- **Total parts** — sum of ratio numbers.
- **[Proportion](../glossary/proportion.md)** — one part compared to the whole.
- **Share** — one portion after splitting in ratio.

### Worked example 1

In a class, the ratio of boys to girls is 2 : 3. What fraction of the class are girls?

Total parts = $2 + 3 = 5$. Girls are 3 parts out of 5.

**Answer:** $\dfrac{3}{5}$ of the class are girls.

### Worked example 2

Share £40 in the ratio 1 : 3. What fraction of the total is the smaller share?

Total parts = 4. Smaller share = 1 part = £10.

Fraction of total = $\dfrac{10}{40} = \dfrac{1}{4}$.

Or directly: smaller share is 1 part out of 4 total parts → $\dfrac{1}{4}$.

### Tips for practice

- Always add ratio parts first for “fraction of the whole”.
- Do not confuse “ratio 1 : 2” with “half” — half is 1 part out of **2** total parts only when the ratio is 1 : 1.
- Check: all fractions of the whole should add to 1.
- For money shares, you can find the fraction before or after working out amounts.
- Three-part ratios (2 : 3 : 5) still add all parts for the whole denominator.
- Draw a bar split into equal blocks — one block per ratio part — to see fractions visually.
