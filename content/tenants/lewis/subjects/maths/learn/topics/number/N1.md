### What this is about

Ordering numbers means putting them in a line from smallest to largest (or the other way round). You compare two numbers at a time using “less than”, “greater than”, or “equal”.

Whole numbers are easy on a number line: further right means bigger. **Negative** numbers are smaller than zero and sit to the left of zero. So $-5$ is less than $-2$, even though 5 is bigger than 2.

[Decimals](../glossary/decimal.md) use place value. Compare digit by digit from the left: tenths before hundredths. $0.3$ is greater than $0.29$ because 3 tenths beat 2 tenths.

[Fractions](../glossary/fraction.md) can be trickier. Same denominator? Compare numerators. Different denominators? Convert to a common denominator or to decimals — then order.

You use ordering when sorting scores, temperatures, or prices — anywhere you need “which is best” or “which is coldest”.

### Words to know

- **Integer** — a whole number, positive, negative, or zero.
- **[Decimal](../glossary/decimal.md)** — a number with a decimal point; each place is tenths, hundredths, and so on.
- **[Fraction](../glossary/fraction.md)** — part of a whole; top number is how many parts, bottom is how many equal parts in the whole.
- **Less than ($<$)** — the number on the left is smaller.
- **Greater than ($>$)** — the number on the left is larger.
- **Equal ($=$)** — both values are the same.
- **Ascending order** — smallest to largest.
- **Descending order** — largest to smallest.

### Worked example 1

Put $-4$, $2$, $-1$, and $0$ in order from smallest to largest.

Draw a mental number line. The most negative is $-4$, then $-1$, then $0$, then $2$.

**Answer:** $-4$, $-1$, $0$, $2$.

### Worked example 2

Which is larger: $\dfrac{3}{5}$ or $\dfrac{7}{12}$?

Convert to decimals or use a common denominator. $\dfrac{3}{5} = 0.6$. $\dfrac{7}{12} \approx 0.583…$

Since $0.6 > 0.583…$, **$\dfrac{3}{5}$ is larger**.

### Tips for practice

- For negatives, think “cold on a thermometer” — further below zero is smaller.
- With decimals, line up the decimal points and pad with zeros ($0.7 = 0.70$).
- Do not compare fractions by “bigger top number wins” unless the bottoms match.
- Say your order out loud: “smallest … biggest” to catch slips.
- On a number line sketch, mark each value before you write the final list.
- When mixing types (fraction and decimal), convert to one type first — usually decimals for ordering, fractions when exactness matters.
