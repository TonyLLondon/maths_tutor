### What this is about

An **[estimate](../glossary/estimate.md)** is a rough answer that is close enough to be useful — often before you calculate exactly, or to spot a silly mistake.

**Round** numbers to friendly values (nearest 10, 100, or one significant figure), then do the calculation mentally. $39 \times 21 \approx 40 \times 20 = 800$.

**Check** an answer using **inverse operations**. If $7 + 6 = 13$, then $13 - 6 = 7$. If $156 \div 4 = 39$, then $39 \times 4$ should be 156.

[Rounding](../glossary/rounding.md) for estimates is not always the same as rounding for reporting — you choose numbers that make mental maths easy.

Estimating helps shopping (“about £30 for six £4.99 items”) and judging if a calculator answer is believable.

### Words to know

- **[Estimate](../glossary/estimate.md)** — approximate value; not exact.
- **[Rounding](../glossary/rounding.md)** — replace a number with a nearby simpler one.
- **Significant figures** — first few digits that matter in a rough number.
- **Inverse check** — undo the last operation to return to the start.
- **Sensible** — in the right ballpark, not exact.

### Worked example 1

Estimate $502 \div 9$ by rounding to easy numbers.

Round 502 to 500 and 9 to 10: $500 \div 10 = 50$.

Exact answer is about 55.7 — 50 is a reasonable estimate.

### Worked example 2

Estimate $58 \times 32$ by rounding each to the nearest 10.

$60 \times 30 = 1800$. (Exact: 1856 — close.)

Check $3456 + 789 = 4245$ by subtracting: $4245 - 789 = 3456$. ✓

Round 6.28 to one decimal place: look at second decimal (8 ≥ 5) → **6.3**.

### Tips for practice

- Round **both** numbers in × and ÷ before estimating.
- One significant figure: first non-zero digit, rest zero (487 → 500).
- Always ask: “Is my exact answer near my estimate?”
- Use inverse checks right after you finish a calculation.
- Estimating division: round divisor and dividend together so the quotient is sensible.
- If an estimate is 800 and your calculator shows 8000, you likely missed a decimal point.
