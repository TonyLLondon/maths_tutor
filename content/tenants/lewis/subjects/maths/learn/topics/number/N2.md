### What this is about

The **four operations** are addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. You combine them to solve real problems: totals, change, equal groups, and sharing.

**Place value** means each digit’s worth depends on its column. In 4732, the 7 is worth 700 (hundreds), not 7. Moving a digit one column left multiplies its value by 10; one column right divides by 10. That is why $3.6 \times 10 = 36$ and $450 \div 100 = 4.5$.

You also work with **negative numbers**. Adding a negative is like going down on a number line; subtracting a negative is like adding.

Column methods help with big numbers: line up digits, carry or borrow when a column goes past 9, and keep decimal points aligned.

### Words to know

- **Add / sum** — put amounts together.
- **Subtract / difference** — take one amount away from another.
- **Multiply / product** — repeated addition or scaling (“times as many”).
- **Divide / quotient** — split into equal groups or “how many times one fits in another”.
- **Place value** — worth of a digit from its position (ones, tens, hundreds…).
- **[Decimal](../glossary/decimal.md)** — numbers with a fractional part after the point.
- **Inverse** — the operation that undoes another (add ↔ subtract, multiply ↔ divide).

### Worked example 1

Work out $5023 - 1876$ using columns.

Start from the ones: borrow as needed. $5023 - 1876 = 3147$.

Check by adding: $3147 + 1876 = 5023$. ✓

### Worked example 2

What is the value of the digit 8 in 3840?

The 8 is in the **hundreds** column, so its value is **800**.

Work out $34 \times 27$ by splitting: $34 \times 20 = 680$, $34 \times 7 = 238$, total **918**.

### Tips for practice

- Line up decimal points before adding or subtracting decimals.
- For $a - (-b)$, rewrite as $a + b$.
- Use multiplication to check division: if $156 \div 4 = 39$, then $39 \times 4$ should be 156.
- Say place value out loud: “3 thousands, 8 hundreds…”
- When multiplying by 10, 100, or 1000, count how many zeros and shift every digit — do not just “add a zero” without thinking about decimals.
- Long division: estimate how many times the divisor fits into the first chunk of the dividend.
