### What this is about

A **power** tells you how many times to multiply a number by itself. $5^2$ means $5 \times 5 = 25$. We call 25 a **square number** because you can make a square of dots with side length 5.

$2^3$ means $2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8$ — a **cube** number. The small number (index) sits up high: in $4^2$, the index is 2.

A **square root** undoes squaring. $\sqrt{49} = 7$ because $7^2 = 49$. A **cube root** undoes cubing: $\sqrt[3]{27} = 3$.

Negative numbers behave carefully: $(-4)^2 = 16$ (negative × negative = positive), but $(-2)^3 = -8$.

You meet powers in area (square units), volume (cube units), and science notation later.

Perfect squares up to $15^2$ save time. Cube numbers for 1–5 help with $\sqrt[3]{\phantom{x}}$ questions. On a calculator, the square-root button undoes squaring for non-perfect squares — but in this topic you mostly stay with whole-number roots.

### Words to know

- **Base** — the number you multiply (the 5 in $5^2$).
- **Index / exponent / power** — how many times to multiply.
- **Square number** — result of $n \times n$.
- **Square root ($\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$)** — number that squares to give the value inside.
- **Cube root ($\sqrt[3]{\phantom{x}}$)** — number that cubes to give the value inside.
- **[BIDMAS](../glossary/bidmas.md)** — powers are done before × and ÷.

### Worked example 1

Work out $6^2$ and $\sqrt{81}$.

$6^2 = 6 \times 6 = 36$.

$\sqrt{81} = 9$ because $9 \times 9 = 81$.

### Worked example 2

Work out $2^3$ and $\sqrt[3]{64}$.

$2^3 = 8$.

$\sqrt[3]{64} = 4$ because $4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64$.

Work out $(-3)^2 = (-3) \times (-3) = 9$. Note: $-3^2$ without brackets means $-(3^2) = -9$ — brackets matter.

### Tips for practice

- Learn square roots for 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100.
- $0^2 = 0$; $1^2 = 1$.
- Squaring a fraction between 0 and 1 makes it **smaller** (e.g. $(\frac{1}{2})^2 = \frac{1}{4}$).
- Always use brackets for negative bases: $(-4)^2$, not $-4^2$.
- $\sqrt{a \times b}$ is **not** always $\sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b}$ in every level of maths — in this topic stick to perfect squares you know.
- Cubing a negative base keeps the sign negative; squaring clears it.
