### What this is about

Measurements use **standard units** so everyone understands the same amount. Length might be mm, cm, m, or km; mass might be g or kg; capacity ml or litres.

**Convert** by multiplying or dividing by the conversion factor. $1 \text{ km} = 1000 \text{ m}$, so $3.5 \text{ km} = 3500 \text{ m}$.

**Compound measures** combine two units, like **speed** = distance ÷ time (km/h, mph, m/s). **Density** = mass ÷ volume (g/cm³). **Pressure** and other science measures follow the same pattern: one quantity ÷ another.

Pick a unit that fits the object: km for a road trip, cm for a pencil, ml for a drink.

Always write units in your answer — “60” is not enough; say “60 km/h”.

Real life mixes units (buy 500 g flour, recipe in kg) — convert **before** you add masses or compare lengths.

### Words to know

- **Convert** — change to another unit without changing the actual amount.
- **Compound measure** — rate built from two basics (speed, density).
- **Speed** — distance per unit time.
- **Density** — mass per unit volume.
- **[Decimal](../glossary/decimal.md)** — helps convert km ↔ m (×1000 moves the point).

### Worked example 1

Convert 250 cm to metres.

$100 \text{ cm} = 1 \text{ m}$, so $250 \text{ cm} = 2.5 \text{ m}$.

### Worked example 2

A car travels 120 km in 2 hours. Find average speed.

Speed = distance ÷ time = $120 \div 2 = 60$ km/h.

Density: mass 20 g, volume 4 cm³ → $20 \div 4 = 5$ g/cm³.

Convert 90 minutes to hours: $90 \div 60 = 1.5$ hours.

### Tips for practice

- Write the conversion as a fraction you multiply by (e.g. $\dfrac{1 \text{ km}}{1000 \text{ m}}$).
- Check sense: km should be a smaller number than metres for the same distance.
- For speed, keep time units consistent (both hours or both seconds).
- Include units in every step to avoid mixing km with miles.
- Time: 1 hour 30 min is 1.5 h, not 1.30 h when calculating speed.
- Density questions state the formula — write it before substituting numbers.
