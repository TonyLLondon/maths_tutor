## What this is about

A [ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md) compares parts using a colon, like 3 : 5. It might mean “3 red for every 5 blue” or “flour to sugar in a recipe”. The order matters: 2 : 3 is not the same as 3 : 2.

You can **simplify** a ratio the same way you simplify fractions — divide both parts by the same number. Equivalent ratios describe the same relationship (2 : 3 and 4 : 6 match). Ratios can have two or more parts, such as 2 : 3 : 5 for three ingredients.

Ratio notation is a clear language for mixing paint, sharing team positions, or describing “for every … there are …” in stories and science.

## Words to know

- **[Ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md)** — a comparison written with a colon (:).
- **Equivalent ratios** — different numbers, same relationship (1 : 2 and 5 : 10).
- **Simplest form** — no common factor bigger than 1 for all parts.
- **Parts** — each number in the ratio counts as one part of the mix.
- **Order** — first number matches the first quantity named in the question.

## Worked example 1

There are 15 apples and 10 oranges. Write the ratio apples : oranges in simplest form.

15 : 10 → divide both by 5 → **3 : 2**.

## Worked example 2

Fill in the blank: 4 : 7 = 12 : ___.

The first part was multiplied by 3 (4 → 12), so multiply the second: $7 \times 3 = 21$.

**Answer:** 21.

## Tips for practice

- Write the ratio in the **same order** as the words (cats : dogs, not dogs : cats unless asked).
- To simplify, use the highest common factor of **all** parts in a three-part ratio.
- Decimals in a ratio? Scale up to whole numbers (1 : 2.5 → 2 : 5).
- “For every 1 of A there are n of B” often appears as 1 : n or A : B = 1 : n — read carefully.
- Simplifying does **not** change how many items you have in a story problem; it only tidies the numbers.
