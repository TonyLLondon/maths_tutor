## What this is about

Two ratios are **equal** when they describe the same relationship. That is [proportion](../../glossary/proportion.md) in ratio form: 2 : 3 = 8 : 12 because both sides simplify to 2 : 3. You scale a recipe, a map, or a mixture by multiplying **both** parts of a ratio by the same number.

If $\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}$ in value, then often $a : b = c : d$ after matching order. Missing values in a proportion are found by the same multiply-or-divide step on both parts. This is the maths behind “if 3 cakes need 6 eggs, how many eggs for 5 cakes?”

## Words to know

- **[Proportion](../../glossary/proportion.md)** — when two ratios are equivalent.
- **Equivalent ratios** — same relationship, different sizes (1 : 4 and 3 : 12).
- **Cross-multiply** — helpful check: $2 \times 12$ should equal $3 \times 8$ for 2 : 3 = 8 : 12.
- **Scale up / scale down** — multiply or divide both parts equally.
- **[Ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md)** — the pairs you are comparing.

## Worked example 1

Solve 5 : 8 = 15 : $x$.

First part × 3 (5 → 15), so second part × 3: $8 \times 3 = 24$.

**Answer:** $x = 24$.

## Worked example 2

A drink mixes juice and water in the ratio 3 : 7. How much water for 450 ml of juice?

3 parts juice → 450 ml, so 1 part = $450 \div 3 = 150$ ml.  
Water is 7 parts: $7 \times 150 = 1050$ ml.

**Answer:** 1050 ml water.

## Tips for practice

- Whatever you do to the **first** part, do to the **second** part.
- Simplify both ratios — if simplest forms match, they are equal.
- Recipe problems: link **one part** to a real amount first.
- Write ratios in consistent order (people : eggs, not eggs : people mid-question).
- If cross-products are not equal, the ratios are **not** the same relationship.
