## What this is about

When one quantity is always a fixed **multiple** of another, you get a straight-line rule like $y = 4x$. Each time $x$ goes up by 1, $y$ goes up by 4. The [ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md) $x : y$ stays equivalent (1 : 4 if $y = 4x$).

Tables of values, graphs through the origin, and “cost per item” stories all fit this pattern. The number in front of $x$ in $y = kx$ is how many times bigger $y$ is than $x$. Linking ratio, [fraction](../../glossary/fraction.md), and [equation](../../glossary/equation.md) helps you move between words, tables, and graphs.

## Words to know

- **Linear** — forms a straight line when graphed; no $x^2$ term.
- **Rule / [formula](../../glossary/formula.md)** — e.g. $y = 3x$ links $y$ and $x$.
- **Multiplier** — the number $k$ in $y = kx$.
- **[Substitute](../../glossary/substitute.md)** — plug a value of $x$ into the rule to find $y$.
- **Origin** — point (0, 0); many proportion graphs pass through it.

## Worked example 1

$y$ is always 5 times $x$. Complete: when $x = 6$, $y =$ ___.

$y = 5x$ → $y = 5 \times 6 = 30$.

**Answer:** 30.

## Worked example 2

A table shows $x = 2, 4, 6$ and $y = 7, 14, 21$. Find the rule and the ratio $x : y$ in simplest form.

$y \div x$ is always 3.5, so **$y = 3.5x$**.  
Ratio 2 : 7 → divide by 2 → **1 : 3.5** or **2 : 7** in whole numbers.

## Tips for practice

- Check **$y \div x$** on each row of a table — it should be constant for $y = kx$.
- Ratio $x : y$ is not the same as $y : x$ — match what the question asks.
- If the graph goes through (0, 0), a simple $y = kx$ rule is likely.
- “Cost of $n$ items at £p each” → total = $pn$ — linear in $n$.
- Fraction “$y$ is what fraction of $x$?” uses $y/x$ when comparing those two.
