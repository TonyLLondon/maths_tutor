## What this is about

When money, sweets, or ribbon is **shared in a ratio**, the ratio tells you how many **parts** each person gets — not the actual amounts until you know the total.

First add the parts: ratio 2 : 3 has $2 + 3 = 5$ parts in all. Then divide the total by 5 to find **one part**, and multiply by each number in the ratio. Three-way ratios work the same: 1 : 2 : 3 has six parts altogether.

This is how fair sharing works when people contribute different amounts, or when a recipe splits ingredients by weight.

## Words to know

- **Share / divide** — split a total into pieces that match the ratio.
- **Part** — one unit of the ratio; all parts are equal in value.
- **Total parts** — sum of the numbers in the ratio.
- **[Ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md)** — the recipe for how many parts each share gets.
- **Largest / smallest share** — the ratio numbers tell you which is bigger before you calculate.

## Worked example 1

Share £72 in the ratio 5 : 3.

Total parts = $5 + 3 = 8$. One part = $72 \div 8 = 9$.  
Shares: $5 \times 9 = 45$ and $3 \times 9 = 27$. Check: $45 + 27 = 72$.

**Answer:** £45 and £27.

## Worked example 2

Divide 120 g in the ratio 2 : 3 : 5.

Total parts = 10. One part = $120 \div 10 = 12$ g.  
Shares: 24 g, 36 g, 60 g.

**Answer:** 24 g, 36 g, 60 g (middle share 36 g).

## Tips for practice

- Always **add ratio parts first** — skipping this is the most common mistake.
- Simplify the ratio before sharing if you can (4 : 6 → 2 : 3); the shares stay the same.
- If the ratio parts are equal (3 : 3), each share is the same — not double one side.
- For “how much more?”, find both shares then subtract.
- Write a line: total ÷ total parts = value of one part.
