## What this is about

If you enlarge a shape with length [scale factor](../../glossary/scale-factor.md) $k$, every **length** is multiplied by $k$. **Area** is two-dimensional, so it is multiplied by $k^2$. **Volume** is three-dimensional, so it is multiplied by $k^3$.

Doubling edge length ( $k = 2$ ) makes area × 4 and volume × 8. Similar shapes have the same angles and matching length ratios, but areas and volumes follow the squared and cubed rules. This explains why small changes in size change capacity or paint coverage a lot.

If two rectangles are similar and you know the ratio of widths, that ratio is the length scale factor for matching sides. Always match **corresponding** sides — longest to longest, height to height — before you square or cube.

## Words to know

- **Similar shapes** — same shape, lengths in a fixed ratio.
- **Length scale factor** — $k$; multiplies each edge.
- **Area scale factor** — $k^2$.
- **Volume scale factor** — $k^3$.
- **[Area](../../glossary/area.md)** and **volume** — measured in square and cube units (cm², cm³).

## Worked example 1

Two similar triangles have corresponding sides 4 cm and 10 cm. Find the length scale factor and the area scale factor.

Length factor = $10 \div 4 = 2.5$.  
Area factor = $2.5^2 = 6.25$.

**Answer:** length × 2.5; area × 6.25.

## Worked example 2

A model cube has edge 2 cm and volume 8 cm³. The real cube has edge 6 cm. Find the volume scale factor and the real volume.

Length factor = $6 \div 2 = 3$.  
Volume factor = $3^3 = 27$.  
Real volume = $8 \times 27 = 216$ cm³.

**Answer:** 216 cm³.

## Tips for practice

- Find **length scale factor first** — compare matching edges.
- Never use the length factor once for area — square it; for volume — cube it.
- Ratio of areas = ratio of lengths **squared** (e.g. lengths 1 : 3 → areas 1 : 9).
- Check units: cm vs cm² vs cm³ are different kinds of measure.
- A common mistake is thinking doubling sides doubles volume — it multiplies volume by 8.
