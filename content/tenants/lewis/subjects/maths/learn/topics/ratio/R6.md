## What this is about

Some comparisons say **“times as many”** — double, triple, half as much. That is a **multiplier**. You can show it as a [ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md) (1 : 3 means the second is three times the first), as a [fraction](../../glossary/fraction.md) of one amount compared to another, or as “what fraction of the **whole**” when everything is mixed together.

Changing 5 to 15 uses multiplier × 3. “Half as many” from 8 to 4 uses multiplier × $\frac{1}{2}$. Spotting multiplier, ratio, and fraction forms helps in science tables, sport stats, and “before and after” price changes.

## Words to know

- **Multiplier** — the number you multiply by to get the new amount.
- **Times as many** — multiplicative comparison (not just add/subtract difference).
- **Fraction of the total** — one type ÷ all types added together.
- **[Ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md)** — compares two (or more) groups directly.
- **Original and new** — keep track of which value came first when writing the ratio.

## Worked example 1

A team has 6 girls and 4 boys. Write the ratio girls : boys and the fraction of the team that are boys.

Ratio: 6 : 4 → **3 : 2** in simplest form.  
Total = 10. Boys as fraction of total: $\frac{4}{10} = \frac{2}{5}$.

## Worked example 2

A price goes from £40 to £50. Write the multiplier for the increase.

Multiplier = new ÷ old = $50 \div 40 = 1.25$ (or “× 1.25”).

**Answer:** 1.25.

## Tips for practice

- Multiplier = **new ÷ original** for “what did we multiply by?”.
- “3 times as many cats as dogs” → cats : dogs = 3 : 1 (order matches the words).
- Fraction **of the total** needs all groups added on the bottom.
- Do not confuse **difference** (subtract) with **times as many** (divide or multiply).
- Draw two bars labelled “before” and “after” when a story describes growth.
