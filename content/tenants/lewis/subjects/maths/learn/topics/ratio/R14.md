## What this is about

The **gradient** of a straight line tells you how steep it is and how fast $y$ changes when $x$ increases. On a graph, gradient = **rise ÷ run** — change in $y$ divided by change in $x$. A [gradient](../../glossary/gradient.md) of 3 means $y$ goes up 3 for every 1 across.

The same idea appears as a **rate**: kilometres per hour, cost per item, or litres per minute. Lines through the origin often model direct [proportion](../../glossary/proportion.md). Horizontal lines have gradient 0; lines that rise left to right have positive gradient.

When a line does not start at the origin, gradient still uses rise ÷ run between any two points on the line — pick points where the grid is easy to read. Steeper lines have larger gradients; a gradient of 10 climbs faster than a gradient of 2.

## Words to know

- **[Gradient](../../glossary/gradient.md)** — steepness; change in $y$ ÷ change in $x$.
- **Rise and run** — vertical and horizontal steps between two points.
- **Rate of change** — how one quantity changes per unit of another.
- **Positive / negative gradient** — rises or falls as you move right.
- **[Coordinates](../../glossary/coordinates.md)** — ordered pairs $(x, y)$ on a grid.

## Worked example 1

A line passes through (0, 0) and (5, 20). Find the gradient.

Rise = 20, run = 5. Gradient = $20 \div 5 = 4$.

**Answer:** 4.

## Worked example 2

A tank holds water that drains at a steady rate. The volume drops 15 litres in 6 minutes. Find the rate of change in litres per minute.

$15 \div 6 = 2.5$ litres per minute (gradient of volume vs time if graphed).

**Answer:** 2.5 l/min.

## Tips for practice

- Use **the same direction** for rise and run (often “end minus start” for both).
- Run must not be 0 — vertical lines have undefined gradient in basic work.
- “Per item” or “per hour” is a gradient in disguise.
- From $(0,0)$, gradient is simply $y/x$ for any other point on the line.
- Negative gradient: level falls as time increases — common in “amount left” stories.
