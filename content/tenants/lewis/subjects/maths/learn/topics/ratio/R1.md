## What this is about

Every measurement has a **unit** — metres, grams, litres, pounds, hours, and many more. The same length can be written as 1.5 m or 150 cm; the number changes but the real length does not.

**Standard units** are the familiar ones you see on rulers, scales, and bottles. **Compound units** combine two ideas, like miles **per** hour (how far in one hour) or pence **per** kilogram (price for each kg).

Changing units is mostly multiplying or dividing by a fixed number: 1 km = 1000 m, so kilometres to metres means × 1000. Getting this right helps you compare sizes, follow recipes, and read speed limits and train timetables without mixing up big and small units.

## Words to know

- **Unit** — the name of what you are measuring (cm, kg, £, minutes).
- **Convert** — rewrite a measurement in a different unit without changing the amount.
- **Per** — “for each” or “in every one” (60 miles **per** hour).
- **Compound unit** — a rate built from two units, like km/h or £/kg.
- **[Decimal](../../glossary/decimal.md)** — a number with a decimal point, often used when converting metres and kilometres.

## Worked example 1

Convert 2.4 km to metres.

1 km = 1000 m, so multiply by 1000:  
$2.4 \times 1000 = 2400$ m.

**Answer:** 2400 m.

## Worked example 2

A car travels 150 miles in 3 hours. Find the average speed in miles per hour.

Speed = distance ÷ time:  
$150 \div 3 = 50$.

**Answer:** 50 mph (a compound unit: miles per hour).

## Tips for practice

- Ask: “Am I changing to a **bigger** unit or a **smaller** one?” Smaller units → bigger number (multiply); bigger units → smaller number (divide).
- Write the conversion you used (e.g. × 1000 for km → m) so you do not flip multiply and divide.
- Always include the unit in your final answer (m, kg, mph, and so on).
- For “which is longer/heavier?”, convert both to the **same** unit before you compare.
- km/h tells you distance for **one** hour — it is a compound unit, not a length on its own.
