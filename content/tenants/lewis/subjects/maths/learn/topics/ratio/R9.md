## What this is about

A [percentage](../../glossary/percentage.md) is a way to write “out of 100”. 25% means 25 out of every 100, or one quarter. You use percentages for test scores, sales, tax, tips, and “25% off” stickers in shops.

Finding a percentage of an amount often starts with 10% (divide by 10), then builds 5%, 20%, or 15% from that. You also convert between percentages, [decimals](../../glossary/decimal.md), and [fractions](../../glossary/fraction.md), and work out what percentage one number is of another.

Increase and decrease mean add or subtract that slice of the original — the **original** is always “100%” at the start.

You might also work backwards: if a sale price is £36 after 10% off, the £36 is 90% of the original, so one percent is £36 ÷ 90 and 100% is ten times that. Percentages connect tightly to [fractions](../../glossary/fraction.md) and [decimals](../../glossary/decimal.md), so flip between forms when one is easier.

## Words to know

- **[Percentage](../../glossary/percentage.md)** — parts per hundred; symbol %.
- **Percent of** — multiply the amount by the percentage as a decimal.
- **Increase / decrease** — add or subtract a percentage of the **original**.
- **Sale price** — original minus discount (or plus markup).
- **Simple interest** — same percentage of the starting amount each time (one step at a time in basic problems).

## Worked example 1

Find 15% of £80.

10% of 80 = 8.  
5% is half of 10% → 4.  
15% = 8 + 4 = **£12**.

## Worked example 2

A £240 bike is reduced by 25%. Find the sale price.

25% of 240 = $240 \div 4 = 60$.  
Sale price = $240 - 60 =$ **£180**.

## Tips for practice

- To find “what % is A of B?”, calculate $A \div B \times 100$.
- 50% = half; 25% = quarter; 20% = divide by 5.
- After a discount, the new price is **not** 100% unless you undo the change carefully.
- Increasing then decreasing by the same % does **not** always return to the start — work both steps.
- Write % as a decimal for one-step multiply: 12% of 50 → $0.12 \times 50$.
