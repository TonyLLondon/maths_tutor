## What this is about

Sometimes you compare **two separate amounts** and ask: “How big is the first compared with the second?” The answer is often a [fraction](../../glossary/fraction.md): “A is $\frac{1}{3}$ of B” means you would need three copies of A to match B.

This is different from “what fraction of the **whole**”. Here the denominator is the **second** quantity (or the one you are comparing **to**), unless the question says otherwise. The same comparison can also be written as a [ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md) like 2 : 5.

These ideas show up when comparing heights, prices, scores, or lengths — anywhere one number is described relative to another.

## Words to know

- **Numerator** — top of the fraction (the part you are talking about).
- **Denominator** — bottom of the fraction (what you are comparing to).
- **[Fraction](../../glossary/fraction.md)** — part-to-whole or part-to-part, depending on the question.
- **[Ratio](../../glossary/ratio.md)** — another way to write a multiplicative comparison (e.g. 3 : 4).
- **Simplest form** — numerator and denominator with no common factor except 1.

## Worked example 1

A pencil is 12 cm and a pen is 18 cm. What fraction of the pen’s length is the pencil’s length?

Compare pencil to pen: $\frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}$.

**Answer:** $\frac{2}{3}$ of the pen’s length.

## Worked example 2

There are 5 red beads and 20 blue beads. Write the number of red beads as a fraction of the number of blue beads.

$\frac{5}{20} = \frac{1}{4}$.

**Answer:** $\frac{1}{4}$.

## Tips for practice

- Underline **which is the fraction of which** — “red as a fraction of blue” puts blue on the bottom.
- Simplify at the end unless the question asks for a specific form.
- A ratio 2 : 7 can become “the first is $\frac{2}{7}$ of the second”.
- Do not swap numerator and denominator unless the question asks for the reverse comparison.
- If the fraction is greater than 1, the first amount is **larger** than the second (e.g. $\frac{5}{4}$).
