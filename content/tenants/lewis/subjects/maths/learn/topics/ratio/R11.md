## What this is about

A **compound unit** measures how much of one thing happens **per** one of another — speed (distance per time), density (mass per volume), or price per kilogram. You calculate it by dividing: speed = distance ÷ time.

Once you know the rate, you can find missing distance, time, or mass by rearranging: distance = speed × time. Units must stay consistent (metres and seconds for m/s, or convert first).

These rates appear in driving, running, shipping costs, and science labs — anywhere “how much per one unit” matters.

If you know any two of distance, time, and speed, you can find the third. A journey at 60 mph for 2 hours covers 120 miles because distance = speed × time. Compound units are written with a slash (m/s) or the word “per” — both mean division.

## Words to know

- **Rate** — one quantity per unit of another (mph, £/kg).
- **Speed** — distance travelled per unit of time.
- **Density** — mass per unit of volume.
- **Pressure** — force per unit of area (when it appears in questions).
- **Average speed** — total distance ÷ total time for the whole journey.

## Worked example 1

A cyclist covers 45 km in 3 hours. Find the average speed in km/h.

$45 \div 3 = 15$ km/h.

**Answer:** 15 km/h.

## Worked example 2

Rice costs £6 for a 4 kg bag. Find the price per kg. How much for 7 kg at that rate?

Price per kg = $6 \div 4 = £1.50$/kg.  
For 7 kg: $1.50 \times 7 = £10.50$.

**Answer:** £1.50 per kg; £10.50 for 7 kg.

## Tips for practice

- Write the division in words first: “distance ÷ time” or “mass ÷ volume”.
- Carry units through the calculation (km/h, not just 15).
- For two-leg journeys, use **total** distance and **total** time for average speed — not the average of the two speeds unless times are equal.
- Convert units before dividing if one measure is km and time is in minutes, for example.
- “Per” always means divide: £ per kg → pounds ÷ kilograms.
