### What this is about

Right-angled triangles have one 90° corner. The **longest side**, opposite that right angle, is called the **hypotenuse**. If you know two sides, you can often find the third.

**Pythagoras’ theorem** links the three sides: $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$, where $c$ is the hypotenuse. It only works in **right-angled** triangles. Use it for ladders against walls, diagonals across rectangles, and checking if a corner is square.

**Trigonometry** uses the same triangle but focuses on **angles**. For an acute angle $\theta$, remember **SOH CAH TOA**: $\sin\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$, $\cos\theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$, $\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$. Label opposite and adjacent relative to the angle you care about.

Together, these tools let you find missing lengths and angles in slopes, ramps, and navigation problems.

### Words to know

- **Right-angled triangle** — one angle is 90°.
- **Hypotenuse** — longest side, opposite the right angle.
- **Opposite** — side across from your chosen angle.
- **Adjacent** — side next to your angle (not the hypotenuse).
- **Pythagoras’ theorem** — $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ for legs $a$, $b$ and hypotenuse $c$.
- **SOH CAH TOA** — memory aid for sin, cos, tan ratios.
- **Angle of elevation** — angle up from horizontal to something you see.
- **[Formula](../../glossary/formula.md)** — e.g. $\tan\theta = \frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}$.

### Worked example 1

**Task:** A right-angled triangle has legs 9 cm and 12 cm. Find the hypotenuse.

**Steps:**

1. Square the legs: $9^2 = 81$, $12^2 = 144$.
2. Add: $81 + 144 = 225$.
3. Square root: $\sqrt{225} = 15$ cm hypotenuse.

The hypotenuse must be **longer** than either leg.

### Worked example 2

**Task:** In a right-angled triangle, the hypotenuse is 10 cm and the angle at the base is 38°. Find the height (opposite that angle). Give your answer to 1 decimal place.

**Steps:**

1. Use sin because you have hypotenuse and want opposite: $\sin 38° = \frac{\text{opp}}{10}$.
2. Opp = $10 \times \sin 38° \approx 10 \times 0.6157 \approx 6.2$ cm.

If you needed the base instead, you would use cos with adjacent.

### Tips for practice

- Before Pythagoras, check the triangle **has** a right angle.
- The hypotenuse is always the side **opposite** the right angle—never use it as a “leg” in $a^2 + b^2$.
- For trig, draw the triangle and mark O, A, H from the angle given in the question.
- Calculator in **degree** mode unless told otherwise.
- Round only at the end if the question asks for a decimal place.
