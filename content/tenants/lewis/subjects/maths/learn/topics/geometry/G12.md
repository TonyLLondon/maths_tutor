### What this is about

Solid objects around you—dice, tins, balls, boxes—are **3D shapes**. They are not flat like drawings on paper. They have thickness and you can pick them up.

In this topic you learn the words that describe solids: **faces**, **edges**, and **vertices** (corners). You also meet common shapes like cubes, cuboids, prisms, pyramids, cylinders, cones, and spheres. Some solids roll; some stack neatly.

A **net** is a flat pattern that folds up to make a 3D shape. If you can imagine folding the net, you can count faces and see how the solid is built.

Knowing these properties helps you pack boxes, choose containers, and solve puzzles about how many faces or edges a shape has.

### Words to know

- **Face** — a flat or curved surface on a 3D shape.
- **Edge** — a line where two faces meet.
- **Vertex** (vertices) — a corner where edges meet.
- **Cube** — six equal square faces, like a dice.
- **Cuboid** — six rectangular faces (a shoe box).
- **Prism** — two matching end faces and the same cross-section all along (like a Toblerone bar).
- **Pyramid** — a flat base with triangular faces meeting at a point.
- **Sphere** — perfectly round, like a ball; no flat faces.
- **Net** — a flattened-out plan that folds into a 3D shape.
- **Cross-section** — the shape you see if you slice straight through a solid.

### Worked example 1

**Task:** A triangular prism has two triangular ends and three rectangular sides. How many faces, edges, and vertices does it have?

**Steps:**

1. **Faces:** 2 triangles + 3 rectangles = **5 faces**.
2. **Vertices:** Each triangle has 3 corners, and the two triangles share no extra corners when joined—count carefully: **6 vertices**.
3. **Edges:** Along the top of the prism you see 3 edges on one triangle, 3 on the other, and 3 joining them = **9 edges**.

You can check with Euler’s rule: faces + vertices − edges = 2. Here $5 + 6 - 9 = 2$. It works.

### Worked example 2

**Task:** A cube and a square-based pyramid have the same-sized square base. They are glued together on that square face (that face disappears inside). The cube alone has 6 faces and the pyramid alone has 5. How many faces on the combined solid?

**Steps:**

1. Start with $6 + 5 = 11$ faces if they were separate.
2. The shared square is **not** visible anymore, so subtract 2 (one from each shape): $11 - 2 = 9$.
3. The combined solid has **9 visible faces**.

### Tips for practice

- Use a real box or dice when you can—count faces, edges, and corners with your finger.
- When a net folds up, do not double-count the face that gets glued to another shape.
- A **cylinder** has curved surfaces; it is **not** a prism, because a prism’s ends must be flat polygons.
- For prisms, the number of edges is often 3 times the number of sides on each end face—try it on a hexagonal prism.
- Sketch a net and label faces before you count—it stops silly mistakes.
