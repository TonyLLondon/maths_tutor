## What this is about

Circles look simple — a round edge — but maths gives each part a **name**. You learn the centre, [radius](../../glossary/radius.md), [diameter](../../glossary/diameter.md), [circumference](../../glossary/circumference.md), chord, tangent, arc, sector, and segment. These words let you read diagrams quickly and follow later topics about [area](../../glossary/area.md) and length around a circle.

Every point on the edge is the same distance from the centre. That single rule is why wheels roll smoothly and why compasses draw perfect round paths.

Getting the vocabulary right now saves confusion when you meet formulas for $\pi$ later. It also helps in science when you label parts of circular motion or lenses.

When a diagram mixes several parts, say each name aloud as you point to it — centre, radius, chord, and so on. That habit stops you from swapping radius and diameter under time pressure.

## Words to know

- **Centre** — the fixed middle point of the circle.
- **[Radius](../../glossary/radius.md)** — segment from centre to any point on the circle; also the length $r$.
- **[Diameter](../../glossary/diameter.md)** — segment through the centre from edge to edge; length $d = 2r$.
- **[Circumference](../../glossary/circumference.md)** — distance once around the circle.
- **Chord** — straight line joining two points on the circle (does not have to pass through the centre).
- **Tangent** — line that touches the circle at exactly one point.
- **Arc** — part of the circumference between two points.
- **Sector** — “pizza slice” bounded by two radii and an arc.
- **Segment** — region between a chord and its arc.
- **Semicircle** — half a circle split by a diameter.

## Worked example 1

**Task:** A circle has radius $7\,\text{cm}$. Find the diameter.

**Steps:**

1. Diameter is twice the radius: $d = 2 \times 7$.

**Answer:** $14\,\text{cm}$.

## Worked example 2

**Task:** A diameter is $18\,\text{cm}$. Find the radius.

**Steps:**

1. Radius is half the diameter: $r = 18 \div 2$.

**Answer:** $9\,\text{cm}$.

## Tips for practice

- Draw and label your own circle every time — muscle memory beats reading alone.
- A **diameter is a special chord** — it is the longest possible chord and it passes through the centre.
- Do not call the whole circle the circumference; circumference is the **edge length**, like a perimeter.
- A tangent touches at one point and is **perpendicular** to the radius at that point (useful in harder questions).
- Keep units with lengths ($\text{cm}$, $\text{m}$) so radius and diameter are not bare numbers.
