## What this is about

Shapes are not just names — they come with **rules**. Triangles always have three sides and $180°$ inside. Quadrilaterals always have four sides and $360°$ inside, but they split into families: parallelogram, rectangle, rhombus, square, kite, trapezium, and more.

You learn which shapes have parallel sides, equal diagonals, lines of symmetry, and [right angles](../../glossary/right-angle.md). For example, a rectangle has four right angles and diagonals of equal length, while a rhombus has four equal sides and diagonals that often cross at $90°$.

Spotting these properties quickly helps you solve angle questions and explain why a shape **must** or **cannot** be a certain type. It also connects to real objects: doors (rectangles), kites (kites!), and tiles (squares or hexagons).

## Words to know

- **Parallelogram** — opposite sides parallel and equal.
- **Rectangle** — parallelogram with four [right angles](../../glossary/right-angle.md).
- **Rhombus** — parallelogram with four equal sides.
- **Square** — rectangle and rhombus combined: four equal sides and four right angles.
- **Kite** — two pairs of equal **adjacent** sides.
- **Trapezium** — at least one pair of parallel sides (UK usage in this course).
- **Diagonal** — line joining opposite corners.
- **Line of symmetry** — fold line where both halves match.
- **Bisect** — cut exactly in half.
- **Isosceles / equilateral / scalene** — triangle side-length types from G1.

## Worked example 1

**Task:** A quadrilateral has opposite sides parallel and equal, but angles are not all $90°$. Name it and say whether its diagonals bisect each other.

**Steps:**

1. Opposite sides parallel and equal defines a **parallelogram** (not necessarily a rectangle).
2. In any parallelogram, the diagonals **bisect each other** (they cross at their midpoints).

**Answer:** Parallelogram; yes, diagonals bisect each other.

## Worked example 2

**Task:** How many lines of symmetry does an equilateral triangle have?

**Steps:**

1. An equilateral triangle has all sides and angles equal.
2. You can fold through each vertex to the middle of the opposite side and get a matching half.
3. There are three such folds.

**Answer:** $3$ lines of symmetry.

## Tips for practice

- Draw a quick sketch and label parallel sides with arrows.
- A square is always a rectangle and a rhombus; a rectangle is **not** always a square.
- For symmetry, try folding on paper — it beats guessing for kites and isosceles triangles.
- Diagonals of a **kite** are perpendicular; diagonals of a **rectangle** are equal in length.
- When a question says “always, sometimes, never”, test a square, a thin rectangle, and a general parallelogram.
