### What this is about

When architects and designers draw a 3D object on flat paper, they often use **three views** instead of one picture. You look at the object from fixed directions—straight down, straight from the front, and straight from the side.

The **plan** is what you see looking **straight down** from above, like a bird. The **front elevation** is what you see looking at the **front**. The **side elevation** is what you see from one **side**. Each view is a flat 2D drawing.

Different views can look different even for the same object. A cylinder standing on a table looks like a **circle** from above but a **rectangle** from the front. Together, the three views help someone build the right shape.

This skill is like reading a simple map of a model before you make it from cubes or draw it on squared paper.

### Words to know

- **Plan** — the view from directly above.
- **Front elevation** — the view from the front, at eye level to that face.
- **Side elevation** — the view from the side (often the right side).
- **Elevation** — any view from the side or front (not from above).
- **2D shape** — flat, like a square or circle in one view.
- **Solid** — the full 3D object made of cubes or other blocks.
- **Isometric sketch** — a 3D-style drawing (different from plan/elevation, but useful for practice).

### Worked example 1

**Task:** Four identical cubes are stacked in one vertical tower on a table. Describe the plan, front elevation, and side elevation.

**Steps:**

1. **Plan (from above):** You see only the top cube—a **single square**.
2. **Front elevation:** You see **four squares** in a vertical column (one per cube).
3. **Side elevation:** Same as the front—a **column of four squares**, because the tower is the same width from the side.

All three views are different shapes except front and side match here.

### Worked example 2

**Task:** You are told: plan = circle, front elevation = rectangle, side elevation = same rectangle as the front. Name the solid.

**Steps:**

1. A **circle** from above fits a round top—think of a tin standing up.
2. **Rectangles** from front and side match a **cylinder** standing on its circular base.
3. The solid is an **upright cylinder**.

If the front were a triangle, you might think of a cone instead—always match **all three** views.

### Tips for practice

- Decide where “front” means before you draw—stick to that choice.
- For cube stacks, draw the plan first on squared paper—it shows how many cubes wide and deep the base is.
- One view alone is often **not enough**: two different solids can share the same plan.
- Hidden parts do not appear in that view; only what you would see through a window at that angle counts.
- Label each drawing **Plan**, **Front**, and **Side** so you do not mix them up in practice.
