### What this is about

Circles appear everywhere: wheels, plates, clock faces, and round tables. A circle is defined by its centre and how far any point on the edge is from that centre.

The **[radius](../../glossary/radius.md)** is from the centre to the edge. The **[diameter](../../glossary/diameter.md)** goes across through the centre—always **twice** the radius. The **[circumference](../../glossary/circumference.md)** is the distance **around** the circle. The **[area](../../glossary/area.md)** is the space **inside**.

You use $\pi$ (pi), about 3.14, in the key [formulas](../../glossary/formula.md): circumference = $\pi \times d$ or $2\pi r$, and area = $\pi r^2$. If you know one measurement, you can often find the others.

Understanding circles helps with how far a wheel rolls in one turn, the size of a pizza, or fencing a round flower bed.

### Words to know

- **[Radius](../../glossary/radius.md)** ($r$) — centre to edge.
- **[Diameter](../../glossary/diameter.md)** ($d$) — widest distance across; $d = 2r$.
- **[Circumference](../../glossary/circumference.md)** — perimeter of a circle; units like cm.
- **[Area](../../glossary/area.md)** — inside the circle; units like cm².
- **$\pi$ (pi)** — special number ≈ 3.14 linking diameter and circumference.
- **Semicircle** — half a circle (along a diameter).
- **[Formula](../../glossary/formula.md)** — e.g. $C = 2\pi r$, $A = \pi r^2$.

### Worked example 1

**Task:** A circle has radius 5 cm. Find its circumference and area. Use $\pi \approx 3.14$.

**Steps:**

1. Circumference: $2 \times 3.14 \times 5 = 31.4$ cm.
2. Area: $3.14 \times 5^2 = 3.14 \times 25 = 78.5$ cm².

Write both answers with correct units.

### Worked example 2

**Task:** A circle has circumference 62.8 cm ($\pi \approx 3.14$). Find its diameter and area.

**Steps:**

1. Diameter: $d = C \div \pi = 62.8 \div 3.14 = 20$ cm.
2. Radius: $r = 10$ cm.
3. Area: $3.14 \times 10^2 = 314$ cm².

Working backwards from circumference is common in practice questions.

### Tips for practice

- Decide early whether the question gives **radius** or **diameter**—do not mix them in $\pi r^2$.
- Circumference uses **one** $r$ (or $d$); area uses **$r^2$**—squaring matters.
- For a wheel, one full turn travels roughly one circumference along the ground.
- Use the $\pi$ value the question asks for (3.14, calculator $\pi$, or leave in terms of $\pi$).
- A semicircle’s curved part is half the circumference, but its full outline also includes the straight diameter.
