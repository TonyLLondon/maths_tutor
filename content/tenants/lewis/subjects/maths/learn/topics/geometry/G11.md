## What this is about

A **coordinate grid** turns geometry into numbers. Each point has an $x$ (across) and $y$ (up) in [coordinates](../../glossary/coordinates.md). You use the grid to find **distance** between points, the **midpoint** halfway along a segment, and simple moves like reflection in the $x$-axis or $y$-axis.

Many problems stay on horizontal or vertical lines first — count squares like steps on a map. Later you combine horizontal and vertical legs to find straight-line distance (think of a right triangle under the segment).

Grids appear in games, GPS-style maps, and graphs in science. Comfortable reading of $(x, y)$ makes every later graph topic easier.

## Words to know

- **Origin** — point $(0, 0)$ where axes cross.
- **$x$-axis** — horizontal number line; $y = 0$ on it.
- **$y$-axis** — vertical number line; $x = 0$ on it.
- **[Coordinates](../../glossary/coordinates.md)** — written $(x, y)$; $x$ first, then $y$.
- **Midpoint** — point exactly halfway between two endpoints.
- **Horizontal distance** — change in $x$ when $y$ stays the same.
- **Vertical distance** — change in $y$ when $x$ stays the same.
- **Reflection in the $x$-axis** — flip vertically; $y$ changes sign.
- **Reflection in the $y$-axis** — flip horizontally; $x$ changes sign.
- **Segment** — straight line between two points.

## Worked example 1

**Task:** Find the midpoint of $A(4, 1)$ and $B(4, 9)$.

**Steps:**

1. Midpoint averages each coordinate: $x = \frac{4 + 4}{2} = 4$.
2. $y = \frac{1 + 9}{2} = 5$.

**Answer:** Midpoint $(4, 5)$.

## Worked example 2

**Task:** Point $P$ is $(-2, 5)$. Reflect $P$ in the $x$-axis. Give the image.

**Steps:**

1. Reflection in the $x$-axis keeps $x$ the same and changes the sign of $y$.
2. $x$ stays $-2$; $y$ becomes $-5$.

**Answer:** Image $(-2, -5)$.

## Tips for practice

- Always say “$x$ first, then $y$” when you read or write coordinates — swapping them is a common slip.
- For distance along a horizontal or vertical line, subtract the coordinates that **change** and use the positive difference.
- Midpoint formula: average the $x$ values and average the $y$ values separately.
- Plot points on squared paper even when the question gives numbers only — seeing the shape helps.
- When finding the fourth corner of a square on a grid, check two sides are equal and meet at a [right angle](../../glossary/right-angle.md).
