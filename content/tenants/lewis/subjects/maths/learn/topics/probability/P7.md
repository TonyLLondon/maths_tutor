## What this is about

A **possibility space** (or sample space diagram) shows **every** combined outcome when you run two or more simple experiments together — like flipping a coin and rolling a dice, or rolling two dice. A **grid** works well: one result on the rows, the other on the columns, each cell is one equally likely outcome if each part is fair.

For two fair dice there are $6 \times 6 = 36$ ordered pairs, such as (2, 5) meaning 2 on the first dice and 5 on the second. You count cells that match what you want — total 8, both even, same number — and divide by 36 to get the probability.

Diagrams turn “and” questions into counting, which is safer than guessing when totals like 7 or 8 are not equally likely even though each pair is.

## Words to know

- **[Sample space](../glossary/sample-space.md)** — the full set of possible outcomes for a trial or combined trial.
- **Possibility space diagram** — a table or grid listing combined outcomes.
- **[Outcome](../glossary/outcome.md)** — one cell in the grid, such as (heads, 4).
- **Equally likely** — each cell has the same probability when each part is fair.
- **Total (sum)** — for two dice, add the two numbers shown in a cell.
- **[Fair](../glossary/fair.md)** — each face or side has the same chance, so each grid cell is equally likely.

## Worked example 1

A fair coin is flipped and a fair 4-sided dice (faces 1–4) is rolled. Draw the idea of the grid: 2 rows (H, T) and 4 columns (1–4), so **8** cells.

Find $P(\text{heads and a number greater than 2})$.

Greater than 2 on the dice means 3 or 4. Favourable cells: (H,3) and (H,4) — **2** cells out of **8**.

$$P = \frac{2}{8} = \frac{1}{4}$$

## Worked example 2

Two fair dice are rolled. How many outcomes give a **total of 9**?

List systematically: (3,6), (4,5), (5,4), (6,3) — **4** outcomes out of 36.

$$P(\text{total 9}) = \frac{4}{36} = \frac{1}{9}$$

Compare with total 2: only (1,1), so $P = \frac{1}{36}$. Totals are **not** equally likely even though each pair is.

## Tips for practice

- Two dice → **36** outcomes unless the question says something different; do not stop at 21 by forgetting reversed orders.
- Label axes clearly: **first dice / second dice** or **coin / dice** so (2,5) is not confused with (5,2).
- For “same number on both dice”, count the diagonal: (1,1), (2,2), … — six outcomes.
- Spinning the same spinner twice uses a grid of size $n \times n$ if there are $n$ equal sections.
- After counting favourable cells, simplify the fraction $\frac{\text{favourable}}{36}$ (or your total).
