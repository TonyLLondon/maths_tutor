## What this is about

When every result you can get is **just as likely** as every other, we say the outcomes are **equally likely**. That is what we mean by a **fair** coin, dice, or spinner: nobody’s favourite result is secretly easier.

You learn to name all the possible **outcomes** in one go — like heads or tails, or the numbers 1 to 6 on a dice. Then you compare how many outcomes match what you care about (for example “rolling an even number”) with how many outcomes there are altogether. That ratio is the **probability**.

Probabilities sit between 0 and 1. Zero means **impossible**, 1 means **certain**, and values in between mean **possible** but not guaranteed. This idea turns “gut feeling” into numbers you can check, which helps in games, raffles, and simple risk questions.

## Words to know

- **[Outcome](../glossary/outcome.md)** — one result that could happen on a single try (for example “tails” on one coin flip).
- **[Fair](../glossary/fair.md)** — every allowed outcome is equally likely.
- **[Probability](../glossary/probability.md)** — how likely an event is, often written as a fraction, decimal, or percentage.
- **[Random](../glossary/random.md)** — you cannot know the result in advance, but you can still describe how likely each outcome is.
- **Impossible / possible / certain** — chance words matching probabilities 0, between 0 and 1, and 1.
- **Favourable outcomes** — outcomes that count as a “win” or “success” for the question you are answering.
- **Total outcomes** — every equally likely result in one trial.

## Worked example 1

A bag has 2 orange counters and 6 purple counters. You pick one counter without looking, and every counter is equally likely to be picked.

**Step 1:** Count favourable outcomes for orange: 2.  
**Step 2:** Count total outcomes: $2 + 6 = 8$.  
**Step 3:** Probability of orange is $\frac{2}{8} = \frac{1}{4}$.

So there is a one-in-four chance of picking orange. Purple would be $\frac{6}{8} = \frac{3}{4}$, which is more likely because there are more purple counters.

## Worked example 2

A fair twelve-sided spinner has equal sections numbered 1 to 12. What is the probability of landing on a number greater than 9?

Numbers greater than 9 are 10, 11, and 12 — that is **3** favourable outcomes. There are **12** sections in total.

$$P(\text{greater than 9}) = \frac{3}{12} = \frac{1}{4}$$

Compare with landing on exactly 10: only **1** section, so $P(10) = \frac{1}{12}$, which is smaller than $\frac{1}{4}$ because fewer sections match.

## Tips for practice

- Always ask: “Are the outcomes **equally likely**?” If not, you cannot just count counters — you need the actual probabilities given in the question.
- List **all** outcomes for one trial before you count (for a dice: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 — not just the ones you hope for).
- For “more likely than”, compare **fractions with the same denominator** (same total outcomes) when you can.
- Remember: on a **fair** dice, 6 is **not** harder than 2 — each face has probability $\frac{1}{6}$.
- “Not picking red” means every other colour counts; check you used the right total in the denominator.
