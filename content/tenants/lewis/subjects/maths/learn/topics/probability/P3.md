## What this is about

Sometimes you do not know the true probability in advance — you **measure** it by doing an experiment. **Relative frequency** tells you what fraction of your trials gave a particular result: $\text{relative frequency} = \frac{\text{number of times it happened}}{\text{total number of trials}}$.

If a coin shows heads 18 times in 24 flips, the relative frequency of heads is $\frac{18}{24} = 0.75$. That is an **estimate** of $P(\text{heads})$ from those 24 flips. It might not be exactly $\frac{1}{2}$ if the coin is fair, because short runs can look lopsided.

The more trials you do, the more the relative frequency usually **settles near** the true probability (for a fair coin, near 0.5). Relative frequency is how sports stats, quality checks, and surveys turn real counts into estimated chances.

## Words to know

- **Relative frequency** — successes divided by total trials; an experimental estimate of probability.
- **[Probability](../glossary/probability.md)** — the true chance if the setup is fair or fully known.
- **Trial / experiment** — one repeat; many repeats give you data.
- **Estimate** — a value guessed from data, not necessarily exact.
- **Percentage** — relative frequency $\times 100\%$ when the question asks for a percent.
- **[Random](../glossary/random.md)** — individual results vary, but the overall fraction can still stabilise with enough data.

## Worked example 1

A factory checks 250 light bulbs and finds 8 faulty.

Relative frequency of faulty $= \frac{8}{250}$.

Simplify: $\frac{8}{250} = 0.032$ as a decimal, or $\frac{4}{125}$ as a fraction.

You could say about 3.2% of that sample were faulty — an estimate for the batch, not a promise for every single bulb.

## Worked example 2

A basketball player scores on 34 of her last 40 free throws. Use this to estimate $P(\text{score})$.

Relative frequency $= \frac{34}{40} = \frac{17}{20} = 0.85$.

So you estimate an 85% chance of scoring on a free throw **based on this sample**. A different week of games might give a slightly different estimate.

## Tips for practice

- Always divide by the **total number of trials**, not by something else (like only the misses).
- Simplify fractions when asked; give **decimals or percentages** when the question specifies.
- A relative frequency of **0** means it never happened in that experiment; **1** means it happened every time.
- Few trials (like 6 flips) can give a relative frequency far from the fair value — that does not always mean the coin is biased.
- When estimating probability from data, say it is an **estimate** from relative frequency, not necessarily the exact true probability.
