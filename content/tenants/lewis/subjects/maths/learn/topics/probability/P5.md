## What this is about

A **sample** is the set of trials or people you actually observe — 10 coin flips, 400 survey answers, or 2000 spinner spins. **Sample size** is how many you included. Bigger samples usually give **more reliable** estimates of probability from relative frequency, because one lucky streak matters less when it is diluted among many trials.

A coin that shows heads four times in four flips gives relative frequency 1 for heads, but that does **not** prove the coin always lands heads. With 400 flips, you would expect a fair coin to land near half heads — and a weird result would stand out more clearly.

You learn to compare small and large samples, judge when an estimate is shaky, and understand that even huge samples give **estimates** that may still differ slightly from the true probability — they just tend to be closer.

## Words to know

- **Sample size** — number of trials or people in your data.
- **Relative frequency** — $\frac{\text{successes}}{\text{sample size}}$; used to estimate probability.
- **Reliable estimate** — usually more trustworthy when the sample size is larger (for the same kind of experiment).
- **[Random](../glossary/random.md)** — fair selection or fair trials so the sample reflects what you want to know.
- **Population** — everyone or everything you care about; the sample is a part of it.
- **[Probability](../glossary/probability.md)** — the true underlying chance; estimates from data approximate it.

## Worked example 1

Two friends estimate $P(\text{heads})$ for the same coin. Alex flips 12 times and gets 9 heads. Bea flips 300 times and gets 158 heads.

Alex’s relative frequency $= \frac{9}{12} = 0.75$.  
Bea’s relative frequency $= \frac{158}{300} \approx 0.527$.

Bea’s estimate is likely **closer to 0.5** for a fair coin because her sample size is much larger. Alex’s 0.75 could easily be luck in only 12 flips.

## Worked example 2

A teacher asks 8 pupils what their favourite subject is; 5 say maths. She says “the probability a pupil prefers maths is $\frac{5}{8}$ for the whole school.”

The sample size is **8**, which is **small** for a whole school. The estimate might be useful as a rough class snapshot, but a survey of 200 pupils spread across year groups would usually give a **more reliable** picture of school-wide preferences.

## Tips for practice

- State the **sample size** when you give an estimate from data.
- Bigger sample → usually **more reliable**, but never guaranteed to hit the exact true probability.
- One trial (one dice roll) cannot give a useful estimate of $P(4)$ — you need many rolls.
- When comparing surveys, compare **sample sizes** and whether the people picked represent the group you care about.
- Quality checks on 5 items from a shipment of 5000 tell you very little about the whole shipment unless the 5 were chosen fairly and you know how sampling works — often the question wants you to say the sample is **too small**.
