## What this is about

**Enumeration** means listing every possibility you care about — without skipping any and without writing the same one twice. **Systematic** means you use a clear order: fix the first choice and walk through all second choices, then move to the next first choice. That is how you count outfits, menu combinations, codes, and later the outcomes of two-stage experiments.

When events are independent and you pick one option from each stage, the total number of combined outcomes is often **multiply**: 3 shirts and 4 trousers give $3 \times 4 = 12$ outfits. Listing them in a table or ordered list proves the count and helps with probability.

Getting the full list right matters because probability is “favourable divided by total”. Miss one outcome and your denominator is wrong.

## Words to know

- **Systematic list** — all outcomes in a fixed order so none are missed.
- **[Outcome](../glossary/outcome.md)** — one full result (for example “soup and pasta”).
- **Independent stages** — choice on stage two does not change the options on stage one.
- **Without repetition** — once a digit or letter is used, it cannot be used again in the same code.
- **Order matters / order does not matter** — AB and BA may count as different or the same; read the question carefully.
- **[Sample space](../glossary/sample-space.md)** — the complete set of all possible outcomes (often built by listing systematically).

## Worked example 1

A lunch deal: one soup (tomato, mushroom) and one roll (white, brown, seeded). List all meals systematically.

Tomato with white, tomato with brown, tomato with seeded;  
Mushroom with white, mushroom with brown, mushroom with seeded.

That is $2 \times 3 = 6$ meals. No pair is repeated, and none is missing.

## Worked example 2

How many two-digit numbers can you make using digits 4, 5, and 6 **without** repeating a digit?

Fix the tens digit first:

- Tens 4: 45, 46  
- Tens 5: 54, 56  
- Tens 6: 64, 65  

There are **6** numbers. (If you only listed 45, 46, 54 you would under-count.)

## Tips for practice

- Use **columns or branches**: all outcomes starting with H before you switch to T for two coin flips (HH, HT, TH, TT — four outcomes, not three).
- For “choose one from A and one from B”, a **two-column list** or grid keeps you organised.
- When **order does not matter** for pairs like {A,B}, AB and BA are the same pair — do not count both unless the question says order matters.
- “Digits may repeat” means 44 is allowed; “without repetition” means it is not.
- After listing, **count** the lines — that total is often your denominator for equally likely outcomes.
