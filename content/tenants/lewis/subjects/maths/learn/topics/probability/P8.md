## What this is about

Real questions often involve **two or more events** in one story: flip a coin **and** roll a dice, or spin twice, or pick from a bag then spin. **Combined events** means finding probabilities when several things happen in one round or in sequence.

Two main rules (when the setup fits):

- **AND** (both happen): for **independent** events, **multiply**: $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)$.
- **OR** (one or the other, not both): for **mutually exclusive** events, **add**: $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)$.

Tree diagrams and sample-space grids both show combined outcomes. Use the grid when you need to count; use multiply when the question gives separate probabilities and independence is clear.

## Words to know

- **[Independent events](../glossary/independent-events.md)** — one result does not change the probability of the other.
- **Mutually exclusive** — cannot both happen on the same trial; use add for OR.
- **Combined event** — two or more parts treated together (both heads, red then blue, six and six).
- **[Sample space](../glossary/sample-space.md)** — list or grid of all combined outcomes.
- **Tree diagram** — branches for each stage; multiply along a path for AND.
- **[Probability](../glossary/probability.md)** — multiply or add only when the rules match the situation.

## Worked example 1

A fair coin is flipped twice. Find $P(\text{two heads})$.

Each flip: $P(H) = \frac{1}{2}$. Flips are independent.

$$P(H \text{ and } H) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$$

The four equally likely outcomes are HH, HT, TH, TT — one is HH.

## Worked example 2

A bag has $P(\text{red}) = 0.2$ on one pick. A separate spinner has $P(\text{win}) = 0.35$. Pick from the bag **and** spin once; the two are independent. Find $P(\text{red and win})$.

$$P = 0.2 \times 0.35 = 0.07$$

For **mutually exclusive** events “small prize” with $P = 0.12$ and “big prize” with $P = 0.03$ on one draw where you cannot win both:

$$P(\text{small or big}) = 0.12 + 0.03 = 0.15$$

Do **not** add $P(\text{six on dice}) + P(\text{head on coin})$ for “six or head” in one coin-and-dice trial — both can happen together, so those events are not mutually exclusive.

## Tips for practice

- Say the rule out loud: **independent AND → multiply**; **mutually exclusive OR → add**.
- For two fair dice rolled once, “sum is 7” is one event — use a **grid**, not $\frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6}$.
- Three heads in a row on fair coins: $\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 = \frac{1}{8}$.
- Tree diagram: multiply along each path; add paths that match the question if several branches work.
- “At least one” often means **1 minus** “none”: $P(\text{at least one head in two flips}) = 1 - P(\text{TT})$.
