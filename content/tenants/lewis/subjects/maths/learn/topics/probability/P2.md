## What this is about

If you know the probability of an event, you can predict **about how many times** it should happen when you repeat the trial many times. That prediction is called the **expected** number of outcomes (or expected frequency).

You multiply the probability by the number of trials: $\text{expected} = P(\text{event}) \times \text{number of trials}$. For a fair coin flipped 100 times, you expect about 50 heads because $0.5 \times 100 = 50$. Real results wobble around that — you might get 47 or 53 — but the expectation gives you a sensible target.

Expected values help you plan: how many raffle winners in a big draw, how many rainy days in a month if each day has the same chance of rain, or how many green pens you might pick if you choose and replace many times. They do **not** promise an exact count on a small number of tries.

## Words to know

- **[Probability](../glossary/probability.md)** — the fraction or decimal chance of one success on a single trial.
- **Expected outcome / expected frequency** — the average number of successes you would predict over many repeats.
- **Trial** — one go at the experiment (one flip, one roll, one pick).
- **[Fair](../glossary/fair.md)** — equally likely outcomes, so theoretical probability matches the fair fraction you calculate.
- **With replacement** — after each pick you put the item back, so probabilities stay the same every time.
- **Theoretical probability** — the “perfect maths” chance before you run the experiment.

## Worked example 1

A fair dice is rolled 90 times. How many sixes would you expect?

On one roll, $P(6) = \frac{1}{6}$.

Expected sixes $= \frac{1}{6} \times 90 = 15$.

So you expect about 15 sixes. Getting exactly 15 is not guaranteed, but 15 is the sensible prediction.

## Worked example 2

A game spinner has probability $\frac{2}{5}$ of landing on “win”. It is spun 75 times.

Expected wins $= \frac{2}{5} \times 75$.

Compute: $\frac{2}{5} \times 75 = 2 \times 15 = 30$.

You would expect about 30 wins in 75 spins. If someone got 70 wins, that would be far from expected and might mean the spinner is not fair — but a short run of only 8 spins could be luck either way.

## Tips for practice

- Use **the same probability** each time when the question says “pick and replace” or “fair” repeated trials.
- Impossible events have expected count **0**; certain events expect **all** the trials.
- Expected values can be **decimals** — round sensibly if the question asks for a whole number of people or items.
- Do not confuse “I **expect** 10 heads in 20 flips” with “I **will** get exactly 10”.
- If you know expected wins and total spins, you can **recover** the probability: $P = \frac{\text{expected}}{\text{trials}}$.
