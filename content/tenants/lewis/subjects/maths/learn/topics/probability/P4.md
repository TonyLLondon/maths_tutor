## What this is about

When you split a single trial into groups, two ideas keep your probabilities honest. **Mutually exclusive** means the groups **cannot both happen at once** on the same trial — rolling a 2 and rolling a 5 on one dice roll cannot both occur. **Exhaustive** means the groups **cover every possible result** with nothing left out — on one coin flip, heads and tails together cover everything.

If events are mutually exclusive and exhaustive, their probabilities **add to 1**. That is why $P(\text{not } A) = 1 - P(A)$ when “$A$” and “not $A$” are the only two possibilities. You use addition for “**or**” when the options cannot overlap: $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B)$.

These rules stop double-counting and help you find missing probabilities — for example the chance of green on a spinner when you already know red and blue.

## Words to know

- **Mutually exclusive** — cannot happen together on one trial.
- **Exhaustive** — together they include every possible outcome.
- **[Outcome](../glossary/outcome.md)** — one result on a single trial.
- **[Probability](../glossary/probability.md)** — a number from 0 to 1 for each event.
- **Complement / “not”** — the event that is everything except $A$; written $P(\text{not } A)$.
- **$P(A \text{ or } B)$** — probability that at least one of $A$ or $B$ happens when they cannot both happen.

## Worked example 1

A spinner has only red, yellow, and blue sections. $P(\text{red}) = 0.15$ and $P(\text{yellow}) = 0.42$. The three colours are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. Find $P(\text{blue})$.

All three probabilities add to 1:

$$P(\text{blue}) = 1 - 0.15 - 0.42 = 0.43$$

Blue has probability 0.43.

## Worked example 2

On one fair dice roll, are “rolling a 6” and “rolling an even number” mutually exclusive?

Even numbers on a dice are 2, 4, 6. Rolling a 6 **is** an even number, so both can happen on the same roll.

They are **not** mutually exclusive. You **cannot** add $P(6) + P(\text{even})$ to get $P(6 \text{ or even})$ without correcting for the overlap.

For “odd **or** even” on one roll, those two events **are** mutually exclusive and exhaustive, so $P(\text{odd}) + P(\text{even}) = 1$.

## Tips for practice

- Draw or list outcomes to see if two events **overlap**. Overlap means they are not mutually exclusive.
- “Rain or no rain” for tomorrow is usually exhaustive with two options — use $1 - P(\text{rain})$.
- Three or more mutually exclusive exhaustive events still add to 1: $P(X) + P(Y) + P(Z) = 1$.
- “Drawing a King” and “drawing a Heart” on one card **can** both happen (King of Hearts) — not mutually exclusive.
- Check the question: “or” in everyday English sometimes means “and/or”; in these rules it means **one or the other, not both** when adding.
