## What this is about

In statistics, the **population** is the whole group you care about — every pupil in a school, every customer in a shop, every plant in a field. Often you cannot measure everyone, so you collect a **sample**: a smaller group chosen to stand for the population. A good sample is **representative** (mix of people, places, or times). A bad sample — only your friends, only one club, only people who volunteer — can be **biased** and mislead you.

You use sample results to make an **inference**: a careful guess about the population. Bigger samples usually give more reliable guesses, but no sample is perfect.

When you **compare two groups**, do not stop at “which mean is higher?” Two datasets can have the **same mean** but different **range** (spread). One group might be steady; another might swing from very low to very high. Report **typical value** (mean or median) **and** spread (often range) together.

If one extreme value distorts the mean — ages mostly in the 30s with one 90-year-old — the **[median](../../glossary/median.md)** may describe “typical” better than the mean.

## Words to know

- **Population** — the entire group you want to learn about.
- **Sample** — part of the population that you actually measure or survey.
- **Bias** — when the sample does not fairly represent the population.
- **Inference** — a conclusion about the population based on sample data.
- **[Mean](../../glossary/mean.md)** — average; sensitive to very high or low values.
- **[Median](../../glossary/median.md)** — middle value; often better when outliers appear.
- **[Range](../../glossary/range.md)** — how spread out the values are.

## Worked example 1

Two classes sit the same quiz.

- Class P: mean 72, range 6  
- Class Q: mean 72, range 28  

**Similarity:** both classes have the same mean score (72 on average).

**Difference:** Class P’s small range means marks are close together; Class Q’s large range means some pupils scored much higher or lower than others. A teacher might say Class P was more **consistent**.

## Worked example 2

Garden A sunflower heights (cm): 88, 90, 89, 91, 90.

Mean: $(88 + 90 + 89 + 91 + 90) \div 5 = 448 \div 5 = 89.6$ cm (about 90 cm).

Range: $91 - 88 = 3$ cm.

Garden B: 70, 95, 88, 92, 55.

Mean: $(70 + 95 + 88 + 92 + 55) \div 5 = 400 \div 5 = 80$ cm.

Range: $95 - 55 = 40$ cm.

Garden A is more **consistent** (small range). Garden B has a wider spread even though some plants are taller.

Street ages: 34, 36, 35, 33, 37, 34, 90, 35. The 90 is unusual. The mean is pulled up; the **median** (35) describes a typical resident better.

## Tips for practice

- Compare **two numbers** for each group when you can: an average **and** a measure of spread.
- Ask “Who was asked?” — chess club only, morning arrivals only, or random pupils from every year?
- Larger samples are usually more trustworthy than tiny ones, but method matters more than size alone.
- Same mean does **not** mean the groups are identical — draw or imagine the spread.
- When writing conclusions, say what the sample was and what you infer about the wider population.
