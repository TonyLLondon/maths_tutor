## What this is about

Lists of numbers appear everywhere: quiz marks, daily steps, temperatures. **Averages** summarise a list with one “typical” value. The **mean** is what you get if you share the total equally. The **median** is the middle value when the numbers are in order. The **mode** is the value that appears most often. The **range** is not an average — it measures **spread**: highest minus lowest.

Different averages tell different stories. The mean uses every number, so one very large or very small value can pull it up or down. The median is steadier when there is an odd “outlier”. The mode fits best when you care about the most common category or score, such as shoe size.

The **distribution** of a list is how the numbers are spread out — bunched near the middle, skewed high, or flat. Two classes can share the same mean but have very different spreads. Always think about **typical value** and **spread** together when you describe data.

## Words to know

- **[Mean](../../glossary/mean.md)** — add all values, divide by how many there are.
- **[Median](../../glossary/median.md)** — middle value in an ordered list (or mean of the two middle values if there is an even count).
- **[Mode](../../glossary/mode.md)** — most frequent value; a list can have more than one mode or none if all values differ.
- **[Range](../../glossary/range.md)** — largest value minus smallest value.
- **Ordered list** — numbers written from smallest to largest before finding the median.
- **Outlier** — a value much bigger or smaller than the rest.

## Worked example 1

Find the mean, median, mode, and range of: 4, 7, 7, 9, 13.

**Mean:** total $4 + 7 + 7 + 9 + 13 = 40$. Count is 5. Mean $= 40 \div 5 = 8$.

**Median:** ordered list is already 4, 7, 7, 9, 13. Middle value is 7.

**Mode:** 7 appears twice — more than any other — so mode is 7.

**Range:** $13 - 4 = 9$.

## Worked example 2

Four numbers have a mean of 15. Three of them are 12, 14, and 18. Find the fourth number.

If the mean is 15 across 4 numbers, the total must be $15 \times 4 = 60$.

Known total: $12 + 14 + 18 = 44$.

Missing number: $60 - 44 = 16$.

Check: $(12 + 14 + 18 + 16) \div 4 = 60 \div 4 = 15$.

For an even-sized list — 3, 5, 8, 11 — the median is halfway between the two middle values: $(5 + 8) \div 2 = 6.5$.

## Tips for practice

- **Order the list** before finding the median; do not confuse median with mode.
- Show mean working: add, then divide — writing the total helps avoid slips.
- The mean of whole numbers is not always whole (for example mean of 1 and 2 is 1.5).
- If every value is different, there is no mode — that is normal.
- Use range to describe **consistency**: a small range often means scores stay close together.
