## What this is about

Once you have frequencies, you can show them in different **charts and tables**. Each type suits a different job. **Bar charts** are brilliant for comparing categories. **Pie charts** show each category as a slice of a whole circle — good for “out of everyone, what fraction chose this?” **Line graphs** (often called line charts) join points to show how something changes **over time**, such as sales each month.

A **two-way table** sorts data by **two** features at once, like boys and girls crossed with who likes football. Row totals, column totals, and the grand total should all agree if nobody is counted twice or missed.

**Grouped frequency tables** put measurements into **class intervals** (for example heights from 130 cm up to but not including 140 cm). They tidy long lists, but you lose exact values — you only know how many people fell in each band. Intervals must **not overlap**, and a value on a boundary must belong to **one** interval only (follow the rule your table uses).

Choosing a chart is a skill: pie charts show parts of a whole; bar charts are easier for reading exact counts; line graphs show trends through time.

## Words to know

- **[Pie chart](../../glossary/pie-chart.md)** — a circle divided into sectors; the angle of each sector matches its share of the total.
- **[Bar chart](../../glossary/bar-chart.md)** — bars for categories; height shows frequency.
- **[Line graph](../../glossary/line-graph.md)** — points joined by lines, often for data over time.
- **[Frequency](../../glossary/frequency.md)** — how many items fall in a category or interval.
- **Two-way table** — a table with rows and columns for two different classifications.
- **Class interval** — a range such as $10 \leq t < 20$ minutes.
- **Modal class** — the interval with the highest frequency in a grouped table.

## Worked example 1

30 pupils were asked how they travel to school. Walk 12, bus 9, car 6, bike 3.

**Pie chart angles:** the full circle is $360°$. Each degree share is $\text{frequency} \div \text{total} \times 360$.

- Walk: $\frac{12}{30} \times 360 = 144°$
- Bus: $\frac{9}{30} \times 360 = 108°$
- Car: $\frac{6}{30} \times 360 = 72°$
- Bike: $\frac{3}{30} \times 360 = 36°$

Check: $144 + 108 + 72 + 36 = 360°$.

## Worked example 2

A two-way table shows instrument players among 40 pupils:

|        | Plays | Does not play |
|--------|------:|--------------:|
| Girls  | 10    | 14            |
| Boys   | 8     | 8             |

Row totals: girls $10 + 14 = 24$, boys $8 + 8 = 16$. Grand total: $24 + 16 = 40$.

Column totals: play $10 + 8 = 18$, do not play $14 + 8 = 22$. Again $18 + 22 = 40$.

So 18 pupils play an instrument, and 24 pupils are girls.

A pupil’s height is 145 cm. Intervals are $140 \leq h < 150$ and $130 \leq h < 140$. The value 145 belongs in $140 \leq h < 150$ because 140 is included in the lower bound of that interval.

## Tips for practice

- For pie charts: **divide each frequency by the total, then multiply by 360** — not the other way round.
- Line graphs need **equal spacing** on the time axis; bar charts for categories do not need joined lines.
- In grouped tables, write intervals clearly ($<$, $\leq$) so every value fits exactly one row.
- To read exact numbers quickly, prefer a **bar chart** over a pie chart.
- When two-way totals disagree, look for a missed cell or double counting.
