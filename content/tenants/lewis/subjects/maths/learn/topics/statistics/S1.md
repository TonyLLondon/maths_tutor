## What this is about

Before you can learn from data, you need to **collect it** in a clear way. You might run a class survey: favourite sport, pets at home, or how you travel to school. Each answer is one piece of information. If you ask everyone the same question and record every answer, you build a dataset you can trust.

**Tally charts** help you count as you go. You mark groups of five (four lines and a diagonal strike-through), then total the marks. A **frequency table** lists each category and how many times it was chosen — the same counts as a tally, but neat for drawing charts.

**Bar charts** and **pictograms** show categories so you can compare at a glance. Bars should start at zero on the scale so sizes are honest. On a pictogram, check the **key**: one symbol might stand for 1 person or for 5 — half a symbol means half of that amount.

Thinking about **who you ask** is the start of good statistics. If you only ask your best friends, the results might not match the whole class. When you ask a smaller group to stand for a bigger group, that smaller group is a **sample**. Fair surveys try to include different people, not just one tight group.

## Words to know

- **[Tally](../../glossary/tally.md)** — marks used to count choices quickly, often in groups of five.
- **[Frequency](../../glossary/frequency.md)** — how many times a category or value appears.
- **[Bar chart](../../glossary/bar-chart.md)** — a chart with bars whose heights show frequencies.
- **Category** — a word label like “apple” or “walk”, not a measured number.
- **Discrete data** — counts such as number of pets (0, 1, 2…), usually whole numbers.
- **Survey** — asking people questions and recording their answers.
- **Sample** — part of a group chosen to represent the whole group.

## Worked example 1

A teacher asks 24 pupils their favourite season. The answers are: spring (6), summer (9), autumn (4), winter (5).

**Step 1 — Check the total.** $6 + 9 + 4 + 5 = 24$, so every pupil is counted once.

**Step 2 — Find the most popular.** Summer has the largest frequency (9).

**Step 3 — Draw a bar chart.** Label the horizontal axis with the four seasons. Label the vertical axis “Number of pupils” from 0 upward. Draw four bars with heights 6, 9, 4, and 5. Give the chart a title such as “Favourite season”.

**Step 4 — Compare.** $9 - 4 = 5$, so 5 more pupils chose summer than autumn.

## Worked example 2

A pictogram uses one sun symbol = 4 pupils for “walk to school”. Walking has 3 full symbols and half a symbol.

Each full symbol is 4 pupils: $3 \times 4 = 12$. Half a symbol is $4 \div 2 = 2$. Total walkers: $12 + 2 = 14$.

Cycling shows 2 full symbols: $2 \times 4 = 8$ pupils.

Difference: $14 - 8 = 6$ more pupils walk than cycle.

If you converted the same counts to a frequency table, the numbers would match the pictogram exactly — only the display changes.

## Tips for practice

- Add all frequencies when each person picks **one** category; the total should equal how many people answered.
- Always read the **key** on a pictogram and check whether the vertical axis on a bar chart **starts at zero**.
- Use tallies while collecting; switch to a table before drawing a chart.
- For a fair class survey, aim to ask pupils from different tables or groups, not just the people next to you.
- Say whether your data are **categories** (colours) or **counts** (number of books) — that helps you pick table vs bar chart.
