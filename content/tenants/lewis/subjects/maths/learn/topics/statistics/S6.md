## What this is about

A **scatter graph** plots pairs of measurements — each person or object gives one dot. The horizontal axis shows one variable (for example hours of practice) and the vertical axis shows another (test score). You look for a **pattern**: do dots rise from left to right, fall, or scatter with no clear trend?

**Positive correlation** means when one value goes up, the other tends to go up too (taller pupils often have larger shoe sizes). **Negative correlation** means one goes up while the other tends to go down (more rain often means fewer park visitors). **No correlation** means no clear linear pattern — dots look like a cloud.

A **line of best fit** is a straight line that follows the trend. It does not have to pass through every dot, or through any dot at all. You balance points **above and below** the line. Use the line to **estimate** missing values: reading between known values is **interpolation**; guessing far beyond the data is **extrapolation** and is often less reliable.

**Correlation is not causation.** Two things can rise together because a third factor affects both (hot weather can increase ice cream sales and swimming visits). An **outlier** sits far from the main pattern — note it, but do not let one dot drag the whole line unless you have a good reason.

## Words to know

- **[Scatter graph](../../glossary/scatter-graph.md)** — a graph of dots, one per pair of values.
- **Correlation** — how closely two variables follow a trend together (positive, negative, or none).
- **Line of best fit** — a straight line showing the overall trend.
- **Gradient** — steepness of the line; on a best-fit line, “change in $y$ per step of 1 in $x$”.
- **Outlier** — a point much away from the others.
- **Interpolation** — estimating using values **inside** the range you measured.
- **Extrapolation** — estimating **outside** the measured range.

## Worked example 1

A line of best fit passes through $(0, 20)$ and $(8, 60)$ (hours studied vs marks).

**Gradient:** change in $y$ is $60 - 20 = 40$ when $x$ changes by $8 - 0 = 8$. Gradient $= 40 \div 8 = 5$. So each extra hour is linked to about 5 more marks on this line.

**Estimate for 5 hours:** when $x = 5$, $y = 20 + 5 \times 5 = 45$ marks. This is **interpolation** because 5 is between 0 and 8.

## Worked example 2

Car age (years) vs value (thousands of £). The trend line goes through $(1, 22)$ and $(9, 6)$.

Gradient: $(6 - 22) \div (9 - 1) = -16 \div 8 = -2$. On average the line drops by about £2000 per year — **negative correlation** (older cars tend to be worth less).

Estimate value at 5 years: halfway in $x$ from 1 to 9 is not always halfway in $y$, but on this straight line, $x = 5$ gives $y = 22 + (-2) \times (5 - 1) = 22 - 8 = 14$, so about £14 000.

If one car is very cheap because it was damaged, that dot might be an **outlier**. Describe it in words, draw the line through the main cloud, and do not assume damage applies to every car.

## Tips for practice

- Label both axes with units (cm, hours, £) before plotting.
- Strong correlation means dots stay close to a straight trend; weak correlation looks more scattered.
- Do not say “A causes B” just because a scatter graph slopes — think of other explanations.
- Prefer **interpolation** over far **extrapolation** when you estimate from a line.
- Sketch the line with roughly equal points above and below; outliers can sit off the line.
