## Getting started

1. Which group has more children: a group of 5 or a group of 8?
2. What is the highest number in: 3, 7, 5?
3. What is the lowest number in: 9, 4, 6?
4. Find the range of: 2 and 8.
5. Class A has an average score of 6 and Class B has an average score of 9. Which class scored higher on average?
6. Everyone in a small group scores 4, 4, and 4. What is the typical score?
7. You ask 3 friends their favourite sport. Does this tell you about every pupil in the school? Answer yes or no.
8. Which set is more spread out: 5, 5, 5 or 1, 5, 9?
9. Find the mean of 2, 4 and 6.
10. Find the median of 3, 1 and 2.
11. In the list 2, 2, 5, 5, 5, which average is the most common value?
12. A sample of 20 pupils is taken from a school of 200. Is this the whole population or a sample?

## Reasoning (AO2)

25. Mia says that comparing only the means of two data sets is enough to fully describe how they differ. Explain what she is missing.

26. **True or false?** A larger sample size generally gives a more reliable estimate of a population's characteristics.

27. Ravi surveys only his friends to find out the most popular sport in his year group. Explain why this sample might be biased.

28. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If two data sets have the same mean, they have the same spread of values.

29. Priya says the mode is the best average to use for numerical data with a big outlier (an unusually high or low value). Explain when the median might be a better choice instead, using an example.

30. Explain why a data set with a small range and a data set with a large range, but the same mean, might have very different real-world meanings — give an example (such as delivery times or exam scores).

31. Sloan says a sample of 5 people is always enough to describe an entire population accurately. Explain why this is not necessarily true.

32. Explain what it means to make an "inference" about a population from a sample, using an example about estimating the average screen time of teenagers from a survey of 50 teenagers.

## Problem (AO3)

33. Two classes' maths test scores are compared. Class X: mean 68, range 15. Class Y: mean 68, range 40. Describe one similarity and one difference between the two classes' performance.

34. A gardener records the heights (cm) of 5 sunflowers from Garden A: 150, 152, 148, 151, 149. Garden B: 130, 170, 145, 155, 100. Find the mean and range for each garden, and describe which garden's sunflowers are more consistent in height.

35. A survey of 8 people's ages in one street gives: 34, 36, 35, 33, 37, 34, 90, 35. Explain why the mean might not be a fair way to describe the "typical" age here, and suggest a better average to use.

36. A company wants to know the average commute time for all its 1000 employees. They survey 30 employees chosen at random from different departments. Explain why this method of choosing the sample is better than only asking employees from one department.

37. Two runners' race times (seconds) over 5 races are compared: Runner A: 12.1, 12.3, 12.0, 12.2, 12.4 (mean 12.2, range 0.4). Runner B: 11.5, 13.5, 11.0, 12.5, 12.5 (mean 12.2, range 2.5). Both have the same mean time. Explain which runner is more consistent, and why a coach might prefer the more consistent runner.

38. A school wants to estimate how many pupils walk to school. Explain why asking only pupils who arrive before 8am might give a misleading result.

## Stretch

39. A population of exam scores has a mean of 60 and a median of 55. Explain what this difference between the mean and the median suggests about the shape of the distribution (hint: think about whether a few very high scores could pull the mean up).

40. Explain why increasing the sample size from 10 to 200 people generally makes an estimate about a population more reliable, but does not guarantee it will be perfectly accurate.

41. A researcher wants to compare the "typical" income in two towns. Town A has a few extremely high earners; Town B does not. Explain why the median income might be a fairer comparison than the mean income in this case.

42. Design your own scenario comparing two groups (e.g. two sports teams, two classes, or two towns). Describe what data you would collect, which averages and measures of spread you would compare, and what conclusions you might be able to draw about the wider population.
