## Getting started

1. Continue the square numbers: 1, 4, 9, __.
2. Continue the odd numbers: 1, 3, 5, __.
3. Continue the even numbers: 2, 4, 6, __.
4. Continue by doubling: 1, 2, 4, __.
5. Find $2^2$.
6. Find $3^2$.
7. Which is a square number: 6 or 9?
8. The sequence is 1, 1, 2, 3, 5. Add the last two terms to find the next term.

## Reasoning (AO2)

23. Sam says 36 can't be a square number because it's even. Explain why Sam is wrong.

24. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The sum of two square numbers is also a square number.

25. Priya notices that adding two consecutive triangular numbers gives a square number, e.g. $3 + 6 = 9$. Test this with the next pair, $6 + 10$, and comment on the pattern.

26. Out of the square numbers and the cube numbers, which number (other than 1) appears in both lists first? Show your working.

27. **True or false?** Every cube number is also a square number. Give an example to support your answer.

28. Leo says the Fibonacci sequence is formed by doubling each term. Explain Leo's mistake and state the correct rule.

29. Compare the 5th square number and the 5th triangular number. Which is bigger, and by how much?

30. Mo says square numbers always end in the digits 1, 4, 5, 6, 9, or 0. Test this using the first ten square numbers — is Mo correct?

## Problem (AO3)

31. A pattern of dots forms triangular numbers: row 1 has 1 dot, row 2 has 2, row 3 has 3, and so on. How many dots are needed to build the 7th triangular pattern?

32. A cube-shaped box is built from unit cubes, with $n$ cubes along each edge. How many unit cubes make up a box with edge length 5?

33. Archer arranges square tiles into growing square patterns: $1 \times 1$, $2 \times 2$, $3 \times 3$, … How many tiles are in the 9th pattern?

34. Sloan's savings double every week, starting at £2 in week 1. How much does she save in week 5 (that week's amount only)?

35. A shop stacks cans in a triangular display: 1 can on top, 2 in the next row, and so on. How many cans are needed for a display with 8 rows in total?

36. Find the difference between the 10th and 9th square numbers, and explain the pattern you notice in the differences between consecutive square numbers.

## Stretch

37. Show that the sum of the first $n$ odd numbers equals $n^2$ by checking it for $n = 1, 2, 3, 4$.

38. Find the smallest number greater than 1 that is both a square number and a triangular number.

39. The Fibonacci sequence starts 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, … Find the ratio of the 6th term to the 5th term, giving your answer to 2 decimal places.

40. Design your own "special" sequence rule that is not simply "add the same number each time" — you could use squares, cubes, or a Fibonacci-style rule. Give the first five terms and explain the rule.
