## Getting started

1. A toy comes in red or blue. List the two possible colours.
2. You can choose a cat or dog and a small or large bed. How many different choices are there?
3. List all the two-digit numbers you can make using 1 and 2 once each.
4. A coin can land heads or tails. How many possible results are there?
5. List all whole numbers greater than 2 and less than 6.
6. Sam can pick apple, pear or banana. How many choices does Sam have?
7. A spinner has A and B. It is spun twice. Which list is complete: AA, AB, BA, BB or AA, BB?
8. How many different pairs can you make by choosing one shirt from 2 shirts and one hat from 2 hats?
9. A drink is water or juice. How many drink choices are there?
10. List both results when a coin is flipped once: heads and ___.
11. A badge is round or square. How many shapes are there to choose from?
12. A code uses the digit 1 or 2. List both possible digits.

21. List all the multiples of 6 between 1 and 40.

## More fluency

## Reasoning (AO2)

27. Sloan lists outfits by only counting shirts, forgetting to multiply by the number of shorts. Explain why this gives the wrong total, and describe the correct method.

28. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If there are 3 choices for a starter and 4 choices for a main course, there are 12 possible meals.

29. Archer says listing the outcomes of flipping 2 coins gives 3 outcomes: 0 heads, 1 head, 2 heads. Explain why this is wrong and give the correct list.

30. Explain a systematic way to list all the two-digit numbers with digit sum 9, without missing any.

31. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The number of ways to arrange n different items in a row is n × (n − 1) × (n − 2) × … × 1.

## Problem (AO3)

34. Archer, Sloan and two friends want to sit in a row of 4 chairs. In how many different orders can they sit?

35. A café menu has 4 sandwich fillings and 3 types of bread, but tuna cannot go on brown bread. How many different sandwiches are possible?

36. How many different 3-digit codes can be made using the digits 0–9, if repeats are allowed and the first digit cannot be 0?

## Reasoning (AO2)

98. Priya lists two-coin outcomes as “both same” and “both different”. Explain why this is incomplete and give the full list.
99. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The number of outfits from 4 shirts and 3 trousers is found by adding 4 and 3.
100. Explain a systematic way to list all two-digit numbers with digit sum 10 without missing any.
101. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The number of ways to arrange $n$ different items in a row is $n!$.
102. Archer groups die outcomes as low (1–2), mid (3–4) and high (5–6). Explain why this is wrong for equally likely outcomes.
103. **Always, sometimes, or never?** A tree diagram with two stages always has the product of branch counts at the leaves.
104. Sloan lists factors of 36 as 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 and 9. What factors are still missing?
105. Explain why listing AB and BA as the same outcome is wrong when order matters for a race.
106. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If you pick one item from each of two lists, a two-way table shows every pair exactly once.
107. Kim lists two-digit numbers from digits 1 and 2 as 12 only. What numbers did Kim forget?
108. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Systematic listing always gives the same total as multiplying the numbers of independent choices.
109. Explain why “at least one head” is not the same as listing HH, HT and TH when two coins are flipped.
110. **Always, sometimes, or never?** From digits 1, 2 and 3 with no repeats, there are $3 \times 2$ different two-digit numbers.
111. A pupil lists permutations of A, B, C as ABC, BCA and CAB only. How many orders are missing?
112. **Always, sometimes, or never?** A rule that forbids some pairs always reduces the total below the full product-rule count.
113. Explain a systematic way to list all factors of 48 using factor pairs.
114. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Listing multiples of 6 by adding 6 each time will list every multiple of 6.
115. Why is listing “0 tails, 1 tail, 2 tails” different from listing HH, HT, TH and TT for two coins?
116. **Always, sometimes, or never?** A sample space diagram for spinners with 3 and 4 sections has 12 cells.
117. Archer lists two-digit codes from digits 1 and 2 as 12 and 21 and says there are 2. When would there be 4 codes instead?
118. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The first digit of a two-digit number can be 0 when digits may repeat.
119. Explain why multiplying the numbers of menu choices works when each choice is independent.
120. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The number of handshakes when every pair shakes once equals $n(n-1)/2$ for $n$ people.
121. Sloan lists outfits by pairing only a red shirt with black shorts. Describe the method that lists every outfit.
122. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Listing outcomes in reverse order after listing forward order counts every outcome twice.
123. Why does choosing 2 toppings from 3 need careful listing, unlike choosing 1 topping from 3?
124. **Always, sometimes, or never?** For equally likely outcomes, grouped categories must still be split into individual outcomes.
125. Explain how fixing the tens digit first helps list all two-digit numbers from three different digits.
126. **Always, sometimes, or never?** A password that allows repeats has at least as many options as one without repeats (same length and symbols).
127. Why can a “no repeats” rule change the count for two-letter codes from two available letters?

## Stretch

151. A restaurant offers 6 starters, 5 mains and 3 desserts. How many three-course meals are possible if you pick one of each?
152. How many five-character passwords can be made from letters A, B and C if letters may repeat?
153. A padlock code uses 4 different digits from 0–9 in order. How many codes are possible?
154. **Always, sometimes, or never?** For $n$ people, handshakes where every pair meets once total $n(n-1)/2$.
155. How many three-digit numbers can be made from digits 1, 2, 3 and 4 without repeating any digit?
156. Four independent choices have 2, 3, 2 and 2 options. How many combined outcomes are possible?
157. Six books are arranged on a shelf, but two books are identical copies. How many distinct arrangements are possible?
158. How many four-letter codes can be made from letters M, N and O if letters may repeat?
159. A flag design picks two different colours in order from five available colours. How many designs are possible?
160. A three-digit code has first digit 1–9 and the other two digits 0–9 with repeats allowed. How many codes are possible?
161. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Choosing 3 items from 5 without order gives fewer outcomes than arranging 3 items from 5 in order.
162. Three distinct prizes are given to three distinct winners (one prize each). How many ways can the prizes be awarded?
163. A sandwich menu has 6 fillings and 2 breads (12 pairs), but 2 pairs are banned. How many sandwiches remain?
164. How many six-digit numbers can be written using only digits 1 and 2?
165. Eight teams each play every other team once. How many games are played?
166. How many three-letter arrangements can be made from S, T, U and V without repeating a letter?
167. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Arranging $n$ different items in a circle gives $n!$ distinct orders.
168. How many two-digit numbers can be made from digits 1, 2, 3 and 4 without repeating digits?
169. A two-character code is one letter A–E followed by one digit 0–9; both may repeat. How many codes are possible?
170. How many pairs of 2 items can be chosen from five items if order does not matter?
171. A five-digit PIN uses digits 0–9 with no repeats. How many PINs are possible?
172. How many distinct arrangements of the letters in the word **MISS** are there?
173. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The product rule still gives the correct count when some combinations are forbidden.
174. How many four-digit numbers can be made using digits 1, 2 and 3 if digits may repeat?
175. Eight runners finish a race. In how many ways can gold, silver and bronze be awarded to three different runners?
176. A menu has 2 starters, 3 mains and 2 desserts (12 meals), but one starter cannot be paired with one main. How many meals remain?
177. A code is two vowels from A, E, I (no repeat) followed by two consonants from B, C, D (no repeat). How many codes are possible?
178. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Listing every cell in a sample space diagram gives the same count as multiplying stage options.
179. How many three-letter codes use letters P, Q, R and S without repeating any letter?
180. A chess club has 10 members; each plays every other member once. How many games are played?
181. How many two-digit numbers greater than 50 can be made from digits 3, 4 and 6 without repeating digits?
182. A password has 3 characters from A–F with no repeats. How many passwords are possible?
183. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If two choices are not independent, you can still use the full product rule without listing.
184. How many ways can 4 different keys be arranged on a key ring if rotations are considered the same? (Hint: fix one key, arrange the rest.)
185. A lunch box picks 2 fruits from apple, banana, cherry and date (order does not matter). How many fruit pairs are possible?
186. How many four-character codes use digits 0–9 or letters A–F (16 symbols) if characters may repeat?
187. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Systematic listing and the product rule always agree when no combinations are excluded.
