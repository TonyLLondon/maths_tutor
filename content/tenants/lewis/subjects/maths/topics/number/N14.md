## Reasoning (AO2)

100. **Always, sometimes, or never?** An estimate from rounding both numbers up before subtracting is bigger than the exact answer.
101. Archer rounds 4.85 to one decimal place and gets 4.8. Explain the mistake and give the correct answer.
102. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Rounding to the nearest 10 makes every number end in zero.
103. Sloan estimates 703 − 298 as 700 − 300 = 400. Is this sensible? Compare with the exact answer.
104. When estimating a product, explain why rounding one factor up and one down can give a closer estimate than rounding both up.
105. **Always, sometimes, or never?** You can use the inverse operation to check whether an addition is correct.
106. A whole number rounds to 50 to the nearest 10. What is the smallest value it could be?
107. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Rounding a positive decimal to fewer decimal places always makes the number smaller.
108. Explain why 49 × 51 is close to 50² even though neither factor equals 50.
109. **Always, sometimes, or never?** An estimate must always be less than the exact answer.
110. Kim says 0.5 rounds to 0 to the nearest whole number. Is Kim correct? Explain.
111. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If you round the same number to the nearest 10 and to the nearest 100, the two answers are always the same.
112. A friend checks 84 ÷ 12 = 7 by calculating 7 × 12. What should they get if the division is correct?
113. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Rounding before adding always gives the same answer as adding exact values then rounding once.
114. Give one situation where a rough estimate is enough and one where you need an exact answer.
115. **Always, sometimes, or never?** 500 is a sensible estimate for 502 + 498.
116. The exact answer is 156. Estimates of 150, 160, and 140 are offered. Which is closest?
117. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Every number has at least one significant figure when written in normal form.
118. Why does rounding 9.95 to one decimal place need extra care?
119. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If an inverse check works once, the original calculation must be correct.
120. Two students estimate 38 × 52 as 40 × 50 = 2000 and 30 × 50 = 1500. Which estimate is closer to the exact product?
121. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Rounding 999 to the nearest 100 gives 900.
122. A length is 6 cm correct to the nearest cm. What is the longest possible actual length?
123. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Rounding both numbers down before multiplying always gives an underestimate.
124. Explain why estimating 99 × 101 as 100 × 100 is useful even though neither factor equals 100.
125. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If an estimate looks wrong, the exact calculation must also be wrong.
126. Archer says you should always round up when estimating costs so you do not run out of money. Do you agree? Explain briefly.
127. **Always, sometimes, or never?** 0.049 rounds to 0.0 to one decimal place.
128. A number rounds to 3.2 to one decimal place. What is the smallest value it could have been?
129. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Dividing by a number very close to 10 makes an estimate using 10 unreliable.
