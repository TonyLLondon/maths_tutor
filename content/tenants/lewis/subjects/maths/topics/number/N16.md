## Reasoning (AO2)

27. Archer says the maximum of $a - b$ is found using the maximum of $a$ and the maximum of $b$. Explain why this is wrong, and state the correct rule.

28. **Always, sometimes, or never?** To find the maximum of $a \div b$, you use the upper bound of $a$ and the lower bound of $b$.

29. Sloan says the minimum of $a + b$ is found using the lower bound of $a$ and the upper bound of $b$. Is she correct? Explain the correct method.

30. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The maximum possible value of a product $a \times b$ uses the upper bounds of both $a$ and $b$.

31. Compare finding the minimum of $a \div b$ with finding the minimum of $a \times b$. Which bounds do you use for each?

## Reasoning (AO2)

101. Archer says the minimum of $a - b$ is found using the minimum of $a$ and the minimum of $b$. Explain why this is wrong and state the correct rule.
102. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The upper bound of $a + b$ equals the upper bound of $a$ plus the upper bound of $b$.

111. Sloan says every error interval uses “≤” at the lower end and “<” at the upper end. Is that the usual rule? Answer yes or no and say why in one sentence.
112. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Bound calculations can give answers with more decimal places than the original measurements.

116. Using the same trip as Q115, find the maximum km per litre, to 1 decimal place.
117. Explain in one sentence why the maximum of $a \div b$ does not use the upper bounds of both $a$ and $b$.
118. **Always, sometimes, or never?** For any positive measurement, the upper bound is greater than the lower bound.
119. A length is 1.25 m, correct to the nearest 0.01 m. Write the error interval.
120. A distance is 45 000 m, correct to the nearest 1000 m. Write the upper bound.
121. **Always, sometimes, or never?** The minimum of $a + b$ uses the lower bounds of both $a$ and $b$.

124. Compare how you choose bounds for the maximum of $a \times b$ and the maximum of $a \div b$.
125. A student writes the error interval for 6 cm (nearest cm) as $5 < x < 6.5$. What mistake did they make?
126. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If you round a bound calculation to fewer decimal places, the true bound can no longer be guaranteed.
