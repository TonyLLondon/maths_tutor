## Getting started

1. A coin is flipped twice. What is the outcome called when both flips are heads?
2. A coin is flipped twice. How many equally likely outcomes are there?
3. A red-or-blue spinner is spun twice. List the four colour outcomes.
4. A coin is flipped and a dice is rolled. Can heads and 3 happen together?
5. If two things must both happen, which probability word joins them: AND or OR?
6. If either one of two separate outcomes can win, which probability word joins them: AND or OR?
7. A certain event happens, then another certain event happens. Is the combined event certain?
8. An impossible event is combined with any other event using AND. Can both happen?
9. A fair coin is flipped twice. How many equally likely outcomes are there?
10. A fair coin is flipped twice. What is P(two heads)?
11. Independent events: P(A) = 0.4 and P(B) = 0.5. Find P(A and B).
12. Mutually exclusive events A and B cannot both happen. To find P(A or B), do you add or multiply the probabilities?

## Fluency (AO1)

13. A coin is flipped and a fair 6-sided dice is rolled. How many outcomes are there in total (use the sample space table method: multiply the number of options)?
14. Two fair coins are flipped. List all 4 possible outcomes in a sample space table.
15. Using your table from Q14, find the probability of getting two heads.
16. Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled and their scores are added. How many total outcomes are possible in the sample space?
17. Using a sample space table for two dice, find the probability that the sum of the two dice is 7.
18. A spinner has sections red and blue, each with probability 0.5. It is spun twice. Find the probability of getting red both times (use the AND rule: multiply).
19. A bag has a probability of 0.3 of drawing a red counter. A separate spinner has a probability of 0.4 of landing on green. Find the probability of drawing red **and** spinning green.
20. Two events are mutually exclusive with probabilities 0.2 and 0.5. Find the probability of one **or** the other happening (use the OR rule: add).
21. A dice is rolled twice. Find the probability of rolling a 6 both times.
22. A coin is flipped 3 times. Find the probability of getting heads all three times.
23. A tree diagram shows two branches for a coin flip, each with probability 0.5, followed by two more branches for a second coin flip, each with probability 0.5. Find the probability of the path Heads then Tails.
24. Events A and B are mutually exclusive. $P(A) = 0.35$ and $P(B) = 0.25$. Find $P(A \text{ or } B)$.

## Reasoning (AO2)

25. Mia says that for two coin flips, there are only 3 outcomes: 2 heads, 2 tails, or 1 of each. Explain what is wrong with her reasoning for finding probabilities.

26. **True or false?** For two independent events, the probability of both happening is found by adding their individual probabilities.

27. Ravi says the probability of rolling a 6 on a dice **or** flipping a head on a coin (in one round of each) should be found by adding $\frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{2}$. Explain why this is wrong, since the two events are not mutually exclusive (they could both happen).

28. **Always, sometimes, or never?** If two events are mutually exclusive, they are also independent.

29. Priya draws a sample space table for two dice being multiplied together (not added). Explain why the table would look different from one for two dice being added.

30. Explain why, on a tree diagram, the probabilities on branches coming from the same point must always add up to 1.

31. Sloan says the probability of an event and its complement (not happening) added together should always equal 1. Is she correct? Explain using an example.

32. Explain the difference between the AND rule (multiply) and the OR rule (add) for combining probabilities, including when each should be used.

## Problem (AO3)

33. A fair coin is flipped and a fair 6-sided dice is rolled. Find the probability of getting a head **and** a number greater than 4.

34. Two fair dice are rolled and their scores are added. Using a sample space table, find the probability that the sum is either 2 or 12.

35. A bag contains 3 red and 2 blue counters. A counter is drawn, replaced, then a second counter is drawn. Find the probability of drawing red both times.

36. A box contains 4 yellow and 6 green balls. A ball is drawn and **not replaced**, then a second ball is drawn. Draw a tree diagram (in words, listing branch probabilities) and find the probability of drawing two yellow balls in a row.

37. A spinner has 4 equal sections: red, blue, green, yellow. It is spun twice. Find the probability of getting the same colour both times.

38. Sloan plays two games. The probability she wins the first game is 0.6, and the probability she wins the second game (independent of the first) is 0.7. Find the probability she wins both games, and the probability she wins neither game.

## Stretch

39. A bag has 5 red and 3 blue counters. Two counters are drawn without replacement. Find the probability that both counters are the same colour (consider both "both red" and "both blue", then add).

40. Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled. Find the probability that the product of the two scores is greater than 20.

41. A box contains 3 defective and 7 working light bulbs. Two bulbs are picked at random without replacement. Find the probability that at least one bulb is defective (hint: find the probability neither is defective, then subtract from 1).

42. Design your own combined-events problem using either a tree diagram or a sample space table (state whether the events are independent or without replacement), and solve it, showing your full method.
