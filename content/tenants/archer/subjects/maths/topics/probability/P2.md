## Fluency (AO1)

1. A fair coin is flipped 20 times. How many times would you expect it to land on heads?
2. A fair six-sided dice is rolled 60 times. How many times would you expect to roll a 6?
3. A spinner has 4 equal sections: red, blue, green, yellow. It is spun 40 times. How many times would you expect it to land on blue?
4. A bag has 3 red counters and 7 blue counters. If you pick a counter and replace it 100 times, how many times would you expect to pick red?
5. The probability that a spinner lands on "win" is 1/5. If it is spun 45 times, how many wins would you expect?
6. A dice numbered 1–6 is rolled 120 times. How many times would you expect an even number?
7. A biased coin has P(heads) = 0.3. If flipped 200 times, how many heads would you expect?
8. In a raffle, 1 in 25 tickets wins a prize. If 500 tickets are sold, how many winning tickets would you expect?
9. A spinner has 8 equal sections numbered 1 to 8. It is spun 32 times. How many times would you expect to land on a number greater than 6?
10. The probability it rains on any day this month is 0.2. In a 30-day month, on how many days would you expect rain?
11. A box has 5 green pens and 15 blue pens. If a pen is picked and replaced 80 times, how many times would you expect green?
12. A fair 10-sided dice is rolled 50 times. How many times would you expect a multiple of 5?

## Reasoning (AO2)

13. Archer rolls a fair dice 30 times and gets a 6 only twice, when he expected 5. Explain why this doesn't mean the dice is unfair.

14. Sloan says, "If I expect 10 heads from 20 flips, I will always get exactly 10." Is Sloan correct? Explain.

15. **Always, sometimes, or never?** An expected outcome is a whole number. Give an example to support your answer.

16. A spinner is expected to land on red 15 times out of 60 spins. Priya says this means P(red) = 1/4. Is she correct? Explain.

17. Mia flips a coin 10 times and gets 8 heads. She says the coin must be biased. Explain why a small number of trials may not prove this.

18. **True or false?** The more times you repeat an experiment, the closer the actual results tend to get, proportionally, to the expected outcomes. Explain.

19. A fair dice is rolled 600 times and lands on 6 exactly 90 times. Is this close to what you would expect from a fair dice? Use the theoretical probability to explain your answer.

## Problem (AO3)

20. A school of 480 pupils each pick a random number from 1 to 4 for a competition. How many would you expect to pick number 3?

21. A factory tests light bulbs; 2% are faulty. In a batch of 3000 bulbs, how many would you expect to be faulty?

22. A bag has red, blue, and green counters in the ratio 1 : 2 : 3, with 12 counters in total. A counter is picked and replaced 240 times. How many times would you expect to pick green?

23. A weather app says 40% of days in a city have rain in October, which has 31 days. How many rainy days would you expect, rounded to the nearest whole day?

24. Two fair dice are rolled together 180 times. How many times would you expect a total of 7? (P(total 7) = 6/36)

25. Sloan buys 3 out of 60 raffle tickets in a raffle with 1 prize, and enters the same raffle 20 times. How many prizes would she expect to win altogether across the 20 raffles?

26. A game has a spinner with P(jackpot) = 1/50. If 4000 games are played, how many jackpots would you expect?

## Stretch

27. Design a probability experiment where you would expect exactly 25 successes out of 100 trials. State the probability of the event and explain your reasoning.

28. A biased coin lands on heads with probability *p*. After 500 flips, you expect 350 heads. Find *p*.

29. Explain why expected outcomes are useful for planning (for example, for a school fair) even though the actual results vary each time you repeat the experiment.

30. A charity raffle sells 250 tickets with 5 prizes. If Archer buys 10 tickets, how many prizes would he expect to win? Explain why this answer can be a decimal even though you cannot win a fraction of a prize.
