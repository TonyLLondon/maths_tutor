## Fluency (AO1)

1. Round 487 to the nearest 10.
2. Round 6.28 to one decimal place.
3. Estimate 39 × 21 by rounding each number to the nearest 10.
4. Estimate 502 ÷ 9 by rounding to one significant figure (or nearest easy numbers).
5. Round 2345 to the nearest 100.
6. Round 8.652 to two decimal places.
7. Estimate 58 × 32 by rounding each number to the nearest 10.
8. Round 0.0847 to two significant figures.
9. Estimate 810 ÷ 42 by rounding to sensible numbers.
10. Round 3.995 to two decimal places.

## More fluency

11. Round 19 872 to the nearest 1000.
12. Estimate 4.8 × 5.1 by rounding each number to the nearest whole number.
13. Round 0.6666 to one decimal place.
14. Estimate 289 + 411 by rounding each number to the nearest 100.

## Reasoning (AO2)

15. A friend estimates 198 + 305 as 200 + 300 = 500. The exact answer is 503. Is the estimate sensible? Explain.

16. **Always, sometimes, or never?** Rounding both numbers up before multiplying gives an estimate that is bigger than the true answer. Give an example.

17. Archer estimates 6.8 × 3.2 as 7 × 3 = 21. Is this a sensible estimate? Work out the exact answer and compare.

18. **Always, sometimes, or never?** When you round a number to the nearest 10, the answer is exactly the same as the original number. Give an example to support your answer.

19. Sloan rounds 3.65 to one decimal place and gets 3.6. Explain her mistake and give the correct answer.

## Problem (AO3)

20. About 31 000 people attend a football match at Wembley. Tickets are roughly £48 each. By rounding sensibly, estimate the total ticket income. Show what you rounded to.

21. A shop delivery contains 288 boxes, and each box weighs about 19 kg. Estimate the total weight of the delivery.

22. Archer's family drove 187 miles on Saturday and 214 miles on Sunday. Estimate the total distance by rounding each to the nearest 10, then compare with the exact total.

23. A cinema has 24 rows with about 32 seats in each row. Estimate the total number of seats by rounding each number to the nearest 10, then explain if your estimate is likely too high or too low.

24. Sloan buys 6 packs of pens costing £2.95 each. Estimate the total cost by rounding the price to the nearest pound, then find the exact cost.

## Stretch

25. Work out 49 × 52 exactly. Then use rounding to estimate the product and compare your estimate with the exact value.

26. A number rounds to 3.8 when rounded to one decimal place. What is the smallest and largest possible value it could have been?

27. Estimate √50 by finding the two consecutive whole numbers it lies between, then say which one it is closer to.

28. A stadium's attendance is reported as "about 20,000, correct to the nearest 1000". Write the smallest and largest possible actual attendance.

## Mixed practice

29. Estimate 612 ÷ 29 by rounding to sensible numbers, then explain if your estimate is bigger or smaller than the exact answer.

30. Archer says rounding a measurement always makes calculations easier but never affects accuracy. Is he correct? Explain.
